Industry Deep Dive

Pakistan's largest export category is textiles/apparel. Karachi hosts the majority of production in SITE industrial area (Pakistan's largest), Korangi, and KITE. 6,000+ registered industrial units. However, factories are actively migrating to Punjab (Gujranwala, Faisalabad, Multan) due to K-Electric's high industrial tariffs (highest in Pakistan) and 6-10 hours daily load shedding. This factory flight costs Karachi tens of thousands of jobs annually.

Root cause: K-Electric's industrial tariffs exceed comparable rates in India, Bangladesh, and Vietnam — making Karachi globally uncompetitive for manufacturing.

GSK Pakistan, Abbott, Hilton Pharma, Searle, OBS, Highnoon, Getz Pharma — nearly all of Pakistan's major manufacturers are Karachi-based. 70%+ of national pharmaceutical production happens here. Exports to 50+ countries. World-class factories with international certifications. Aga Khan University Hospital already draws patients from Afghanistan, Iran, and East Africa for treatments unavailable elsewhere regionally. Conservative medical tourism potential: $500M/year with proper visa facilitation and hospital accreditation programs.

Pakistan's ICT exports: $2.6B in 7 months of FY25/26 (~$3B+ annualized). ~1 million freelancers nationwide. Karachi houses: NayaPay (digital banking), Bykea (logistics), major software outsourcing hubs, 27 airlines at Jinnah International handling 6M passengers/year. IBA Karachi, SZABIST, NED, Karachi University — producing thousands of graduates. But: inconsistent power, poor internet quality, no proper tech park. Lahore's Arfa Software Technology Park is 10 years ahead of Karachi in IT ecosystem. A dedicated Karachi Tech Park could add $1-2B in annual ICT exports.

Karachi has been a software outsourcing hub for Pakistan since the 2000s. It pioneered cable networking in Pakistan. It has the talent. It lacks governance.

Jinnah International Airport (also known as Quaid-e-Azam International) is Pakistan's largest and busiest, handling 6 million passengers per year. 27 airlines fly to Jinnah, predominantly from the Middle East and Southeast Asia. Combined with the two ports, Karachi is Pakistan's only genuine gateway to the world. The airport handles the largest number of foreign airlines in Pakistan.

World Economics estimates Pakistan's informal economy at 34% of total economic activity — contributing to why official GDP figures are underestimates. In Karachi, with unmetered utilities, unregistered businesses, slum manufacturing, street vending, and the entire tanker water economy, the informal share is substantial. The tanker mafia alone (~$317M/day at official estimates) is 100% informal and criminal. Informal workers have zero legal protections, no minimum wage enforcement, no pensions, no health insurance.

"Pakistan's GDP in 2025 is $410.5 billion (IMF). If Karachi contributes 25% of national GDP, and its city budget is $400M — what percentage of its own output does Karachi receive back as municipal funding?"
✅ Correct. Karachi produces ~$102.5B (25% of $410B). Its city budget is $400M = 0.39% of its own output. Compare to Singapore (a city-state) where municipal spending is 6% of GDP, or Mumbai where it's ~0.9%. Karachi retains less than half a percent of what it produces. This mathematical fact explains every infrastructure failure in the city.
💸 Remittances — The $30B Lifeline That Stays Unproductive
Depth Addition — Source: State Bank of Pakistan FY2024, World Bank Remittance Report

Pakistan Received $30.25 Billion in Remittances in FY2024 — Karachi Gets the Largest Share

Pakistan's remittances in FY2023-24: $30.25 billion — the country's second-largest source of foreign exchange, exceeding all foreign direct investment. Karachi, as the largest city and financial hub, receives the largest urban share. But where does remittance money actually go? State Bank data and household surveys consistently show: 45-50% into real estate (plot purchases, housing — often the fraudulent schemes described in Chapter 18). 25-30% into family consumption (food, utilities, school fees). 10-15% into gold. Less than 5% into productive business investment. Why? Because there is no safe, accessible, transparent investment infrastructure for ordinary Pakistanis. The stock market is opaque and volatile. Bank deposit rates are consistently below inflation. Starting a business requires navigating the same corrupt regulatory systems that drove the investor abroad in the first place. Pakistan's diaspora is the country's most important financial lifeline — and the country systematically fails to channel that capital into productive investment. The result: remittances fuel consumption inflation (making Karachi more expensive) and real estate speculation (making housing less affordable) rather than creating jobs or building productive capacity.

Chapter Three

How We Got Here

Every problem you live with was created in a specific decade by a specific decision. Understanding history is the only way to understand power.

1700s
Origin

Kolachi — The Fishing Village

A small Baloch and Sindhi settlement at the Indus estuary. Population: ~14,000. Natural harbor. Known to Arab and Persian traders. The British East India Company takes control of Sindh in 1843 and immediately recognises the strategic value of the harbor.

1843–1947
Golden Era

The British Port City — Greatest Regional Export Hub

By 1914: largest wheat export port in the entire British Empire. British-built canal irrigation in Sindh drove agricultural surplus through Karachi. Victorian-era drainage, sewerage, roads, and port infrastructure built — the last time Karachi's systems truly functioned. Population grows from 14,000 to ~400,000. Much of what KWSB still "operates" today are these same 100-year-old British pipes.

1947
Partition — The Permanent Wound

Millions of Muhajirs Arrive — Original Residents Feel Overwhelmed

Pakistan created. Karachi becomes the first capital. Millions of Urdu-speaking Muhajirs arrive from India — educated, urban, professional, but entirely landless. Original Sindhi and Baloch residents feel their city is being taken. Hindu merchants (who ran much of commerce) emigrate to India. The city's social fabric is permanently altered in months. This tension — Muhajir versus original inhabitants — is the root of every ethnic political conflict in Karachi, and it has never been genuinely resolved.

1958
Capital Moved — The Abandonment

Ayub Khan Moves Capital to Rawalpindi/Islamabad

Military dictator Ayub Khan relocates Pakistan's capital 1,500 km north — next to Army GHQ. Karachi loses political status while retaining economic dominance. Federal investment follows. Infrastructure maintenance funding dries up. The "we generate everything, receive nothing" grievance that defines Karachi's political identity was born on this day and has never been answered.

1972
The Quota System — MQM's Seed

Bhutto's Quota: Only 40% of Govt Jobs for Urban Sindh

PM Zulfikar Ali Bhutto (PPP): only 40% of federal jobs to urban Sindh (mostly Muhajirs), 60% to rural Sindh and elsewhere. Muhajirs who dominated civil service are systematically excluded from the country they helped create. This rage, suppressed 12 years, explodes into MQM in 1984. The single most consequential domestic policy decision for Karachi's political future.

1979
Afghan War — The City Gets Armed

CIA + ISI Funnel Weapons Through Karachi

Soviet invasion of Afghanistan. Billions in CIA weapons and Pakistani military money flow to Mujahideen through Karachi — Kalashnikovs, RPGs, money, and heroin. What was a densely populated but relatively unarmed city becomes one of Asia's most heavily armed civilian populations. Every ethnic conflict, every militant wing, every Lyari gang from the 1990s-2010s was armed from this 1979-1988 pipeline. The 1,742 deaths of 1995 are a direct consequence.

1984
MQM Founded

Altaf Hussain — Student Rights to City-Wide Machine

Muhajir Qaumi Movement founded at Karachi University. Within 5 years: THE dominant force in all urban Sindh. Revolutionary grassroots organization — office in every neighborhood, worker on every block. Initially fought genuine Muhajir rights. Gradually developed torture cells (documented by Human Rights Watch), systematic extortion, targeted killings. For 30 years, being Muhajir in Karachi meant being MQM — no alternatives allowed.

1990–2001
The Killing Decade

1,742 Dead in 1995 Alone — 4.7 Per Day

Karachi becomes urban warzone. MQM vs. PPP's Lyari gangs, Pashtun vs. Muhajir, Sunni vs. Shia. 1995: 1,742 killed. Extortion on every business. Ethnic no-go zones. Factories flee to Punjab. Investment collapses. Karachi ranked among world's most dangerous cities. The economic cost of this decade is impossible to fully calculate — but it set Karachi back 20+ years relative to peer cities.

2013
Rangers Operation

Operation Karachi — Peace With Documented Abuses

Pakistan Rangers target: MQM militant wing, PPP's Lyari gang (People's Aman Committee — Uzair Baloch), Taliban cells, extortionists. Crime drops dramatically. Bhatta effectively ends in most areas. But Human Rights Watch and Amnesty International document: enforced disappearances, extrajudicial killings, torture. Estimated 10,000+ arrested. Security achieved without justice or accountability.

1947–53
First Religious Riots — Forgotten Chapter

1953: Anti-Ahmadi Riots — Karachi's First Post-Partition Religious Violence

In January 1953, Karachi witnessed its first major religious riots following a campaign by religious parties demanding that the Ahmadiyya community be declared non-Muslim. Mobs attacked Ahmadi homes and businesses in Karachi, Lahore, and other cities. The government declared martial law in Lahore. The riots killed dozens and injured hundreds. A judicial inquiry (Munir Commission Report, 1954) remains one of the most penetrating analyses of religion's role in Pakistani politics ever written. Its warnings about the dangers of using religion as political tool went entirely unheeded. The Ahmadi question was "resolved" in 1974 when PM Bhutto's government constitutionally declared Ahmadis non-Muslim — making Pakistan the only country to legally define a religious community out of Islam. The 1953 riots in Karachi were the template for every subsequent instance of religiously motivated violence in the city.

1985
The Bus Burning — MQM's Origin Moment

April 1985: Bushra Zaidi Killing Triggers Ethnic Massacre — Marks Start of MQM Era

On April 14, 1985, a Karachi University student named Bushra Zaidi was killed by a speeding minibus in Liaquatabad. The driver was Pashtun. The student was Muhajir. What followed was three days of ethnically targeted violence: buses driven by Pashtuns were set on fire with passengers inside. Muhajir-owned properties in Pashtun areas were attacked. Over 50 people were killed, hundreds injured, and the city was paralysed. The episode demonstrated the explosive ethnic tinderbox that Karachi had become after the weapons flood of the Afghan War era. It was directly precipitating for MQM's organisational expansion from student movement to street power. Altaf Hussain used the incident to argue that Muhajirs needed their own armed political organisation for self-defence. The Bushra Zaidi riots are the moment Karachi's ethnic conflict became militarised — and the direct origin point of the 1990s killing decade.

1992
First Army Operation Against MQM — Almost Forgotten

June 1992: Operation Clean-Up — The Army Moved Against MQM First in 1992, Not 2013

Most people think the 2013 Rangers operation was the first time the military moved against MQM. It was the second. In June 1992, the Pakistan Army under General Asif Nawaz Janjua launched "Operation Clean-Up" in Karachi — officially targeting criminals and militants. In practice it was a direct military operation against MQM's street infrastructure. MQM offices were raided, weapons caches seized, and party workers arrested. Altaf Hussain fled to London in 1992 — ostensibly for medical treatment, actually to escape the operation. He never permanently returned. The 1992 operation was partially successful in disrupting MQM's street organisation. Then: the operation was ended when MQM agreed to political accommodations with the federal government. This established the pattern that would repeat in 2013: military pressure → political accommodation → MQM continues → military pressure again. The lesson: military operations against politically embedded networks without structural political reform are temporary fixes. MQM recovered from 1992 and became more violent. The same dynamic risk existed after 2013.

Altaf Hussain's incendiary live speech from London inciting violence against Pakistani media. Rangers move on MQM offices. Party fractures: MQM-Pakistan, MQM-London, PSP. August 2025: Altaf Hussain formally dissolves MQM — ending 41 years of Muhajir political organization. The community remains fragmented and politically leaderless.

2023
New Politics

JI Wins Karachi Mayor — First Non-MQM Mayor in Modern History

Jamaat-e-Islami wins local government elections 2023 — a historic upset. Cross-ethnic, religious conservative appeal. First non-MQM mayor of modern Karachi. But: Mayor still has almost no real authority. Water is Sindh's. Police is Sindh's. Budget is Sindh's. A new face. The same broken system.

2025
Today

Record Ports, Record Stock Market — Crumbling City

KPT: record 54M tonnes. KSE-100: record 174,000+. IMF Pakistan GDP: $410.5B. And: 64,323 street crimes. 455 MGD sewage into the sea. K-IV water project still incomplete. MQM dissolved. The paradox of Karachi 2025: never more economically significant. Never more physically neglected. The engine runs. The city crumbles.

Nominal city government. 7 District Municipal Corporations (DMCs) underneath. Mayor cannot control: water (KWSB), police (Sindh), health (Sindh), education (Sindh), ports (Federal), power (K-Electric). Essentially governs parks, markets, and roads — and even those inadequately. Budget ~$400M total, most from Sindh allocations. Wikipedia notes the city faces a "governance crisis bound by provisions of 18th Amendment giving all local body formation powers to provinces with absolutely no room for federation intervention."

The 18th Amendment (2010) was meant to devolve power downward. In Karachi's case, it devolved power to Sindh Province (PPP), not to Karachi city. The city got less power, not more.

The 2025 Auditor General of Pakistan audit is damning: "very little maintenance since 1960." KWSB is bankrupt and deeply corrupt. Non-revenue water: 40-50%. Military-linked authorities control 20-30% of Karachi's most valuable land. It is two separate governance systems existing simultaneously in one city.

Intelligence not shared. Turf conflicts slow response to major crimes. After Rangers took over many police functions (2013-18), regular police became demoralized, further corrupting and further losing capacity. The result: 64,323 street crimes in 2025 with 70 deaths — in a city with two separate law enforcement agencies.

🏗️ Land Mafia — How It Operates
🔴 Source: KDA records, Dawn investigations, Supreme Court suo motu notices

The Land Mafia Operating System — Fake Documents, Political Protection, Violence Against Residents

How land is grabbed in Karachi:

Method 1 — Forged revenue records (Patwari corruption): Pakistan's land records system uses local revenue officers (Patwaris) who can alter ownership records. A plot occupied by a family for 40 years can have its records changed to show a different owner — for a fee. The original residents then face eviction backed by police acting on the forged document. This has been documented in hundreds of cases across Karachi. The Supreme Court has taken suo motu notice of Patwari corruption multiple times.

Method 2 — Katchi Abadi invasion: Informal settlements (katchi abadis) housing millions of Karachi's poor sit on land that residents have occupied for decades but never formally titled. Land grabbers identify valuable katchi abadi land (typically near new development), obtain a fraudulent lease or allotment from a corrupt KDA or land department official, and use police and private thugs to evict residents. The residents have no legal title to fight back with.

Method 3 — Amenity plot conversion: Every housing scheme in Karachi must legally dedicate 30% to parks, schools, and public facilities. Developers routinely sell this land as residential plots after obtaining fraudulent plan amendments from KDA. The parks and schools that Karachi legally should have — and the residents who paid for them — never receive them. This represents thousands of acres of stolen public land across the city.

The political protection dimension: Land grabbing in Karachi historically linked to: MQM's real estate wing (documented in Rangers operation 2013-2018, multiple criminal cases); PPP-linked figures in Lyari and other Sindh-administered areas; military-linked developers (DHA expansions acquired through government orders without civilian oversight). Tier 1 — What You Can Do Alone, Today, Free

Text your 13-digit CNIC number to 8300 from any Pakistani mobile number. You'll get a reply telling you if you're registered and in which constituency. If not registered: visit vote.ecp.gov.pk or any NADRA office with your CNIC. It takes 15 minutes and costs nothing.

Why this matters more than any other single action: Karachi's official census of 14.9 million (vs actual 20M+) is partly sustained because millions of eligible voters are not registered. Every unregistered voter is a political nonentity. The establishment and PPP both structurally benefit from low voter registration in urban Sindh. In 2018, youth voter registration drives added hundreds of thousands of new young Karachi voters — and PTI won 14 of 21 NA seats, shocking a political establishment that had counted on low urban youth turnout.

Pakistan's voter rolls have millions of "ghost voters" in rural constituencies and millions of missing actual voters in urban ones. Both are deliberate. Your registration is one act of direct resistance against this manipulation.

The Pakistan Citizen Portal (PCP) at citizen.gov.pk (or the app on iOS/Android) is directly connected to the PM's office and routes complaints to the relevant ministry or agency. Unlike social media posts that disappear, PCP complaints: (1) receive a case number (2) are legally assigned to a specific official (3) require an official written response (4) track escalation if not resolved (5) create a permanent documented record.

What to report via PCP: broken KWSB pipe/no water supply at your address; illegal water connection (hydrant) near your home; KDA/SBCA safety violation in a building near you; garbage not collected from your street for 3+ days; K-Electric overcharging or no response to an outage complaint; any government school or hospital facility failure.

PCP complaints about KWSB failures have demonstrably resulted in official field visits and responses in some cases — not always, but significantly more often than a tweet. File for every failure, every time. The cumulative data from PCP reports in a specific area gets reviewed in ministry performance dashboards.

File complaints even when you expect nothing to happen. PCP data is reviewed in federal-provincial coordination meetings. A neighbourhood that generates 200 PCP complaints about the same broken water main over 6 months becomes a data point in official performance reviews that individual complaints never generate.

The Sindh Transparency and Right to Information Act 2016 gives you the legal right to request documents from any Sindh government body — KWSB, KDA, SBCA, KMC, Sindh Education, Sindh Health. Agencies are legally required to respond within 21 days. If they don't: appeal to the Sindh Information Commissioner.

High-impact RTI requests for Karachi:

→ KWSB: Request the tender contract for water hydrants in your district. Ask for the flow-meter calibration records. Ask for the Non-Revenue Water audit for your zone. These documents expose the tanker mafia's connection to official contracts.

→ KDA/SBCA: Request the building inspection records and permit approval for any recently collapsed or visibly dangerous building in your area. Specifically request the name of the approving officer.

→ KMC: Request the road repair contract for your street — who was awarded it, how much, when. Compare with the current condition of the road.

→ Sindh Education: Request the inspection reports for your nearest government school. Request the teacher attendance records.

How to submit: Write "RTI Request" on the envelope/email. Address to the "Public Information Officer" of the agency. Include your name, contact, the specific documents, and cite the Sindh Transparency Act 2016. Send by registered post or email. Keep a copy.

Many RTI requests are ignored or delayed — this is illegal under the Act. But the act of filing creates a legal record, and pattern of unanswered RTI requests from multiple citizens creates grounds for a petition to the Information Commissioner, which can result in administrative consequences for the agency.

Every broken main, every garbage pile on a nullah, every flooded road, every collapsed building. Video it. Timestamp it. Note the GPS coordinates (your phone camera does this automatically in photo metadata). Post with tags: @KarachiMetro @SindhCMHouse @KWSBofficial @KDA_Karachi on X/Twitter and Facebook. Add the specific address. Add which agency is responsible.

Why public tagging matters beyond virality: (1) Journalists monitor these government account tags. (2) Officials whose accounts are publicly tagged with failure documentation cannot later claim ignorance. (3) A geotagged, timestamped public video is inadmissible in court as standalone evidence — but it is admissible as supporting documentation. (4) When 50 people tag the same broken pump station in Korangi over 6 months, it becomes a news story. (5) International monitoring bodies (UN rapporteurs, World Bank project teams) track social media documentation of project failures.

The KWSB audit that found "very little maintenance since 1960" was partly triggered by years of accumulated public documentation by residents and journalists that made the official position ("infrastructure is being maintained") impossible to defend. Documentation accumulates into accountability — not immediately, but inevitably.

When you report a crime and police refuse to file an FIR (First Information Report) — which happens constantly in Karachi — you have legal tools:

Step 1: Cite Section 154 of the Code of Criminal Procedure (CrPC) to the officer. This section legally mandates registration of any cognisable offence reported. Tell the officer: "I am citing Section 154 CrPC. You are legally required to register this FIR."

Step 2: If refused, note the officer's name, badge number, and station. Write a formal complaint to the DSP (Deputy Superintendent of Police) of the area.

Step 3: If still refused, file a private complaint with the Judicial Magistrate of your area under Section 200 CrPC. Magistrates can direct police to register FIRs. This is a fully legal process that requires no lawyer for basic cases.

Step 4: File a complaint with the Inspector General of Sindh Police online at sindh.gov.pk/dpt/Police.

Every FIR filed creates an official permanent record. CPLC (Citizens-Police Liaison Committee) uses aggregate FIR data to produce Karachi's annual crime statistics — the same data used in court petitions, policy documents, and international assessments. Your individual FIR is a data point in the system that holds police accountable to performance metrics.
Tier 2 — With a Small Group of 5-20 People
More effort. Dramatically more impact. Every documented community-level success in Karachi — from Orangi's sewerage to Lyari's post-violence recovery programs — started with small groups. Local governments respond to organised constituent pressure the way they never respond to individuals.

Resident Welfare Associations are legally recognised bodies in Pakistan. Most Karachi neighbourhoods have dormant ones. A functioning RWA has legal standing to: file formal collective complaints with KWSB (compelling scheduled tanker supply with capped prices, bypassing individual negotiations); submit documented complaints to KMC about specific road/drainage failures with legal follow-up rights; file RTI requests as an organisation (carrying more institutional weight than individual requests); engage your Union Councillor as an organised bloc of voters rather than individuals; collectively document and refuse extortion attempts (safety in numbers — documented cases exist of organised communities successfully refusing bhatta).

How to form one in one afternoon: Gather 5+ residents. Elect a chairperson, secretary, treasurer by show of hands. Draft a simple two-page constitution (templates available from KMC's community services department). Register with your Union Council — free, takes 30 minutes. Done. You are now a legally recognised body with standing to file collective complaints.

The RWA model works because local governments everywhere respond more to organised constituent groups than to individuals. This is how city governance functions worldwide — not just in Karachi. The unusual thing about Karachi is how few RWAs are actually active despite being legally available.

Karachi has Union Councils — the smallest legally recognised unit of local government. Each has an elected Union Councillor. Most Karachi residents have never met theirs. This is a strategic mistake.

Your UC councillor has: legal standing to escalate complaints to DMC and KMC level; formal access to the KMC complaint resolution pipeline; attendance rights at DMC budget discussions; the ability to officially table a neighbourhood grievance for district-level response.

Find your UC councillor: visit your nearest Union Council office (listed at kmc.gos.pk) or ask at your local neighbourhood. UC office hours are legally required to be public. Show up as a group of 10-15 residents with documented, photographed, timestamped complaints. A UC councillor facing an organised group of voters with formal documented grievances cannot dismiss them without creating a public record of that dismissal — which is itself a future accountability tool.

In the 2023 local elections, organised community groups in specific Karachi neighbourhoods successfully pressured UC candidates to sign written commitments on specific infrastructure issues as a condition of receiving organised voter bloc support. Written commitments from elected officials are legally enforceable as part of their mandate — not as personal contracts, but as documented public positions.

Who Actually Gets Targeted — The Pattern

HIGHEST RISK — documented targeting:

Journalists investigating the military's business interests, enforced disappearances, or intelligence agency operations. Baloch nationalist activists. PTI political workers post-May 2023. Anyone publicly documenting military operations in Balochistan or KP. Activists who lead large public movements against the establishment. People who directly challenge ISI operations publicly.

LOW RISK (but not zero) — the majority of civic activity:

The risk profile of civic engagement in Karachi is highly specific. The vast majority of ordinary civic participation — the kind described in the Take Action chapter — carries very low direct risk. The high-risk activities involve directly challenging the military establishment's interests or operations. These are different things. A Karachite organising their neighbourhood about water supply is NOT in the same risk category as a journalist documenting ISI safe houses.

Perween Rahman (2013): Director of the Orangi Pilot Project. Documented land records and slum residents' land rights — directly threatening land mafia interests who controlled millions in property. Shot and killed by gunmen. Case went to trial. Three men were convicted. The intellectual authors of the killing — land mafia leaders — were never prosecuted. What made her a target: she had documented evidence of specific land grabs by specific named powerful people.

Zeenat Shahzadi (2015): 24-year-old journalist investigating an enforced disappearance case. Abducted by armed men. HRCP believes it was an intelligence agency operation. She was recovered in 2017 near the Afghan border — 2 years later. What made her a target: she was investigating the ISI's role in a disappearance case.

Five Social Media Activists (January 2017): Salman Haider, Ahmad Waqass Goraya, Aasim Saeed, Ahmad Raza Naseer disappeared simultaneously from different Pakistani cities. All were known for criticism of the military establishment on social media. All reappeared weeks later — with no official explanation, no charges filed, no accountability. What made them targets: direct online criticism of military leadership.

Muhammad Siddique Mengal (May 2024): Journalist. Killed by a bomb placed on his vehicle in Balochistan. Had received prior death threats. Local press club president. What made him a target: reporting on militant violence in his area.

Nasrullah Gadani (2024, Sindh — PPP territory): Killed. His family alleges the killing was ordered by a PPP-affiliated lawmaker. This shows that targeting of journalists happens from political parties too, not only the military.

Pattern analysis: The vast majority of targeted journalists and activists had one thing in common — they had documented specific evidence against specific powerful people or institutions. General complaints about bad governance are tolerated. Specific documented evidence naming specific powerful people is what triggers targeting. This distinction matters for how you engage.

The Sindh Maintenance of Public Order (MPO) Ordinance allows Sindh police to detain anyone deemed a "threat to public order" for 30 days without charge, extendable to 6 months. No trial. No FIR needed. No lawyer access for initial period.

In March 2025, Karachi police arrested Baloch activist Sammi Deen Baloch and four others under the MPO while they were protesting outside the Karachi Press Club. The MPO detention order stated their "presence in public can cause a grave threat to people's safety." They were protesting peacefully about enforced disappearances. MPO was used because police had no actual crime to charge them with.

The MPO can be used against anyone organising protests in Sindh. Understanding this law matters: if you organise any public protest or gathering, you are theoretically subject to MPO detention. The law requires only a police officer's opinion that your presence threatens public order — no court approval needed initially.

After MPO detention, the detainee can challenge via the Sindh High Court. Human rights lawyers at organisations like HRCP have successfully challenged MPO detentions. Contact: HRCP helpline 042-35761999. If someone is detained under MPO, immediate contact with a lawyer and public documentation is critical — disappearances are more likely when no one knows a person has been detained.
Practical Safety — What Actually Protects You

The single most important safety principle for activists and journalists in Pakistan, documented across hundreds of cases: people who are well-known publicly are significantly harder to disappear quietly than those who are anonymous.

When Perween Rahman was killed, it caused an international outcry because she was a documented, internationally recognised figure. Her killers went to trial — which almost never happens. The five social media activists who were disappeared in 2017 and released weeks later were relatively well-known voices — their disappearance caused immediate public pressure for release.

Practical steps for visibility protection:

→ Tell people where you're going before attending any meeting with officials, protest, or sensitive interview

→ Document and share your activities publicly — the more people who know what you're doing, the harder it is to silence you quietly

→ Connect with established organisations (HRCP, TI Pakistan, Dawn's journalist network) before doing anything high-risk — they create institutional awareness of your work

→ Never go alone to document sensitive information. Always have a witness

Any sensitive documentation you create — RTI request copies, videos of official failures, records of corruption — should be:

1. Stored in at least two places you control — cloud backup + physical USB at a different location than your home. If your phone is taken, you don't lose the evidence.

2. Shared with at least one trusted person outside your immediate circle — a friend in another city, a journalist contact, an NGO. If something happens to you, the documentation survives.

3. For journalists/high-risk activists: Signal app (encrypted messaging) for sensitive communications. ProtonMail for sensitive emails. VPN when accessing sensitive material. These are not paranoia — they are standard practice for journalists operating in Pakistan. The CPJ, Committee to Protect Journalists, provides a safety kit downloadable at cpj.org/safety.

4. For standard civic activity (RTI, complaints, voting): No special security measures needed. The risk profile for ordinary civic participation does not require encrypted communications.

If someone is arrested or disappears in Karachi:

→ HRCP Emergency: 042-35761999 (24-hour) — Human Rights Commission of Pakistan. Has legal teams that can intervene immediately for detained persons.

→ Lawyers for Human Rights and Legal Aid (LHRLA) Karachi: 021-35830893 — provides free legal help for people detained without charge

→ Edhi Foundation: 021-111-11-33-23 — for missing persons database and family support

→ Voice for Baloch Missing Persons: Active organisation that specifically tracks and documents enforced disappearances — has established legal channels

→ Amnesty International Urgent Action Network: Can issue international alerts within hours for documented detention cases. The faster an international alert goes out, the more difficult a quiet detention becomes for authorities.

→ Sindh High Court: Any citizen can file a habeas corpus petition in the Sindh High Court for a detained person — this is the fastest legal mechanism for a detained person's release. A lawyer is helpful but not legally required.

The most dangerous moment for a detainee in Pakistan is the first 24-48 hours — when family doesn't know where they are and no legal process has started. Immediate public documentation + legal contact = significantly reduced risk of indefinite disappearance.

Pakistan has specific laws that are routinely used against activists and ordinary citizens. Knowing the legal red lines keeps you safer:

PECA (Prevention of Electronic Crimes Act) 2016, as amended 2025: Vague language criminalises online content deemed to "cause fear, panic, disorder" or damage "national security." Has been used to arrest people for tweets criticising the military. Practical rule: criticise policies and systems publicly. Do not post content naming specific military officers or ISI operations.

Blasphemy laws (PPC 295-C): Carries the death penalty. Has been weaponised against individuals in personal disputes. Practical rule: never engage in any religious debate online that could be interpreted as disrespect to religious figures. This is not about free speech — it is about physical survival.

Official Secrets Act: Makes it illegal to possess or share "official secrets." Has been used against people who obtained government documents through leaks. Practical rule: if a source provides you with government documents they were not supposed to share, treat them with extreme caution.

MPO (Sindh Maintenance of Public Order): Used for organising protests. Practical rule: any organised public protest in Sindh should register with local police 48 hours in advance under the Public Order Act — this does not eliminate risk but creates a legal record of lawful intent.

If you are arrested by police or taken by anyone claiming to be law enforcement:

1. Stay calm. Do not resist physically.

2. Ask immediately: "What is the charge?" "Under which law am I being detained?" "I am requesting access to a lawyer." Say these exact words loudly in front of any witnesses present.

3. If you have your phone: Immediately text your location and situation to at least two people before handing over the phone. Even a 3-word message is better than nothing.

4. You have the right to: Know the reason for arrest (Section 55, CrPC), contact a lawyer (Constitutional Article 10A), appear before a magistrate within 24 hours of arrest (Section 61, CrPC), not be tortured (Article 14 of the Constitution).

5. After any detention: Write down every detail — officer names, badge numbers, locations, times, what was said — as soon as you are able. File this with HRCP and a lawyer. This documentation is what makes follow-up accountability possible.

Pakistan's Constitution theoretically prohibits torture, arbitrary detention, and enforced disappearance. These constitutional rights are violated routinely by security agencies. But knowing and asserting your legal rights on record creates a documentary basis for legal challenge — which has successfully secured the release of detained persons in numerous documented Karachi cases.
"The answer to the question 'what can I do if I might get killed for it?' is not silence. It is organised, documented, collective action that makes individual targeting difficult and costly. Lone voices are easy to silence. Organisations with international connections, documented records, and legal representation are much harder."
This is not a guarantee of safety. It is an honest guide to reducing risk while not surrendering civic participation to fear.

🏛️ History Nobody Taught You
Archaeological Fact

🏺 Karachi Has a 4,000-Year-Old History

Most people think Karachi was "founded" in 1729. In reality, archaeological excavations found Indus Valley Civilisation sites around modern Karachi — including Allahdino and Banbhore, dating to 2500-1500 BCE. Alexander the Great camped at Krokola (identified as modern Karachi area) in 326 BCE to prepare his fleet for Babylon after his Indus Valley campaign. Arab traders knew it as "Debal" — the same port from which Muhammad bin Qasim launched his conquest of Sindh in 712 CE. Your city is 4,000 years old, not 300.

The Name Itself

🐟 "Karachi" May Mean Seaport — Named After a Woman

The most widely cited legend: Karachi was named after Mai Kolachi, a fisherwoman. The settlement was called "Kolachi-jo-Goth" (Village of Kolachi). The word "Karachi" is believed to derive from the Sindhi word for seaport. The first documented European record of the name "Karachee" appears in a Dutch ship document from 1742 — a merchant vessel de Ridderkerk that was shipwrecked nearby. The city was formally established as "Karachi" in 1729 when fishermen from the silting-up port of Kharak relocated to the Hub River mouth area.

The Lost Tramway

🚋 Karachi Had a Tram Network for 91 Years — Then Killed It

A tramway service began in Karachi in 1884 — one of the earliest in Asia, before Lahore or Mumbai had comparable services. It ran for 91 years. It was shut down in 1975 — deliberately, as private transport operators lobbied against public transit. Turkey has since offered assistance in reviving a modern tramway. That offer has been pending for years. The tram infrastructure is gone. The political will to replace it: still absent. Karachi had mass transit. It was deliberately removed.

The World Record

🚑 Karachi Hosts the World's Largest Volunteer Ambulance Service

The Edhi Foundation, founded by Abdul Sattar Edhi in 1951, runs the world's largest volunteer ambulance network — a Guinness World Record. It covers all of Pakistan but is headquartered in Karachi. It has an estimated 1,500+ ambulances, mortuaries, homes for the destitute, and orphanages. Edhi himself reportedly lived in the same modest room for 60 years and owned two pairs of shoes when he died in 2016. The service saved millions of lives. The government contributed almost nothing. Citizens built it.

Unreported Geography

🐢 Green Sea Turtles Nest on Karachi's Beaches Annually

Every year, endangered green sea turtles nest on Karachi's Hawksbay and Sandspit beaches. Pakistan has one of the largest green turtle nesting sites in the world. The beaches that host them are also contaminated with 455 MGD of raw sewage daily. WWF Pakistan runs turtle protection programmes but the same beaches tourists use for weekend barbecues are also turtle nesting grounds that are threatened by both sewage and plastic waste. Karachi's coastline hosts incredible biodiversity — largely invisible because the government has never prioritised coastal protection.

The Invisible Port

✈️ PIA Set a Speed Record From Karachi That Still Stands

Pakistan International Airlines (PIA) completed the fastest ever commercial flight from Karachi to London in 1962 — 6 hours, 43 minutes, and 55 seconds. The record still stands. PIA in the 1960s was considered one of the world's finest airlines and was consulted by Emirates, Singapore Airlines, and Korean Air during their founding years. What PIA was in 1965 is what Singapore Airlines is today. What Karachi Port was in 1914 is what Singapore Port is today. The potential was always there.

The Hidden Financial Architecture

Pakistan's Parliament has never received a comprehensive, line-by-line disclosure of CPEC loan terms. What has been established through leaked documents, IMF discussions, and investigative reporting:

Interest rates: CPEC energy sector loans carry rates of 6-7% per annum — significantly higher than comparable World Bank (1-2%) or ADB (2-3%) loans. Commercial loans from Chinese banks: market rates.

Capacity payments: Pakistan's government agreed to purchase electricity from CPEC power plants at guaranteed rates regardless of whether the plants actually generate power. Profit repatriation: Chinese companies operating CPEC projects repatriate profits in dollars. As the rupee depreciated, the dollar cost of these payments increased dramatically — making CPEC increasingly expensive for Pakistan in real terms.

The "collateral" question: Multiple Pakistani economists and opposition politicians have raised concerns about whether CPEC contracts contain infrastructure collateral provisions (as seen in Sri Lanka's Hambantota port). The government has not provided complete documentation to contradict or confirm this.

The IMF's 2024 programme assessment noted CPEC's capacity payments as a significant fiscal stress. The exact terms under which Pakistan would default on CPEC obligations remain classified in Pakistani government records.

CPEC's strategic priority is China's access to the Arabian Sea and Indian Ocean — specifically a route that bypasses the Malacca Strait (China's strategic chokepoint). Gwadar Port, at the mouth of the Persian Gulf, is that access point. Karachi is important as Pakistan's existing economic engine, but it is not China's strategic priority. Gwadar got: a Chinese-operated deep sea port, a new airport (Chinese-funded), a special economic zone, dedicated road infrastructure, and a Chinese residential compound.

Karachi got: projects that were largely Pakistan's own priorities (BRT, motorway) with partial CPEC funding. The projects that would transform Karachi's competitiveness — KCR revival, KWSB modernisation, K-Electric regulatory reform — were not CPEC priorities because they didn't serve China's strategic interest.

Chinese analysts describe CPEC as primarily an energy security and trade route project for China. Pakistani political messaging described it as a development programme for Pakistan. Both were correct — and the gap between these two descriptions is where Karachi's CPEC disappointment lives.

Positives: M-9 Motorway provides a functional Karachi-Hyderabad connection. BRT Green Line, while underperforming, is functional infrastructure that did not exist before. Some industrial connectivity improvements in the port area.

Negatives: The power sector CPEC deals contributed to circular debt that now burdens every Pakistani, including Karachi residents through K-Electric's tariff structure. The focus on Gwadar drew federal infrastructure attention and investment away from Karachi. Chinese workers and contractors on CPEC projects had preferential terms over local Karachi labour in several documented cases.

The honest assessment: CPEC was not a Karachi programme. It was a China-Pakistan strategic programme that intersected with some Karachi infrastructure. Judging CPEC by what it promised Karachi is to judge it by the wrong metric. Judging CPEC by what it promised China: it largely delivered.

Chapter Eighteen — The Plot That Isn't There

The Property Scam

DHA, Bahria Town, and dozens of housing societies in Karachi have sold plots on land they don't legally own. The victims: primarily the overseas Pakistani diaspora who never visited. ₨300B+ in estimated fraudulent transactions.

How It Works — Source: FIA Property Cases, Dawn Investigations

The Plot You Bought May Not Exist

The standard Karachi housing society scam has several variants: (1) Land not legally acquired: Housing societies advertise and sell plots on agricultural or government land that they have not obtained legal title to. When the government reclaims the land, the buyers lose everything. (2) Double-selling: The same plot is sold to two different buyers — the developer keeps both payments, and only one (if any) receives actual possession. (3) File trading: Plots are sold as "files" (booking documents) before development begins. These files are bought and sold speculatively. The underlying land often never gets developed, or gets developed for someone else. (4) Phantom developments: CGI imagery of beautiful neighbourhoods, master plans, brochures — but zero actual construction after years of payment collection. FIA has hundreds of active cases against Karachi housing societies. Convictions: rare. The frauds continue.

Primary Victims — FIA Data

🌍 Overseas Pakistanis Are the Main Targets

The overseas Pakistani diaspora — people who left Karachi, earn in dollars/pounds/euros, and want to invest back home — is the primary fraud target. They cannot visit to verify plots. They trust family members or agents. They cannot easily pursue legal action from abroad. Estimated 30-40% of all fraudulent housing scheme complaints filed with FIA involve overseas Pakistani victims. The people who left to send money home are the people most systematically robbed by the system they are sending money to.

Scale — Estimates

💰 ₨300 Billion+ In Estimated Fraud

Precise figures are impossible because most victims never report (they fear legal costs, or the society has political connections). FIA estimates and advocacy groups suggest hundreds of billions of rupees in fraudulent real estate transactions in Karachi annually. The real estate sector is heavily cash-based, records are opaque, and the political connections of major housing society operators make prosecution rare.

How to Protect Yourself — Verified Checklist

1. Verify the NOC (No Objection Certificate): Any legitimate housing scheme in Karachi requires NOC from KDA (Karachi Development Authority) or SBCA. Demand the NOC number and verify it directly on the KDA website (kda.gos.pk) or at their offices. No NOC = illegal scheme = your money at severe risk.

2. Verify land ownership: The land records for Karachi are maintained by the Sindh Board of Revenue. Request a "fard" (land record extract) for the specific plot. This costs a few hundred rupees and can be obtained from the Board of Revenue office or their online portal (sbr.gos.pk). If the society cannot provide a fard showing they own the land: do not buy.

3. Never pay cash: Always pay by crossed cheque or bank transfer directly to the housing society's registered bank account. Keep all receipts. Cash payments have no legal standing for recovery.

4. Have a lawyer review the agreement: A property lawyer (DHA Bar Association, Karachi Bar Association) can review the sale agreement for standard fraud indicators. Cost: ₨5,000-15,000. On a purchase of hundreds of thousands or millions of rupees, this is the single best investment.

5. If something goes wrong: File with FIA Cyber Crime Wing (fia.gov.pk) and KDA's Complaint Cell. Also file with Sindh Real Estate Regulatory Authority (RERA) — Pakistan's real estate regulatory body established in 2020, still developing enforcement capacity but provides a formal complaint channel.

Political connections of major housing society operators (DHA is military-linked; Bahria Town has connections that have shielded its founder from prosecution despite a Supreme Court order) mean that even legitimate complaints often face institutional resistance. Documentation is your only protection — create a complete paper trail from day one.

Chapter Nineteen — Pakistan's Triangle Shirtwaist

The Baldia Fire

September 11, 2012. 289 workers burned alive. Factory exits locked. 13 years later: zero convictions. This is the most consequential workplace massacre in Pakistan's history — and most people don't know the real story.

The Facts — Documented, Verified, Ignored
289 workers died. Their factory exits were locked from outside. The fire started in circumstances investigators called suspicious. The factory's owners had refused to pay extortion to MQM's industrial wing. The fire started the day after that refusal.

The Ali Enterprises garment factory fire in Baldia Town, Karachi on September 11, 2012 killed 289 workers — Pakistan's deadliest industrial fire and one of the deadliest industrial accidents in world history. It happened 101 years after the Triangle Shirtwaist fire in New York (146 dead, 1911) that transformed American labour law. The Baldia fire transformed nothing in Pakistan. German retailer KiK (a customer of Ali Enterprises) eventually paid €5.1 million in compensation after German court proceedings. The factory owners and the alleged architects of the arson faced Pakistani courts for 13 years — as of 2025, there are still no final convictions.

The Full Story

Initial reporting described the fire as accidental. A Joint Investigation Team (JIT) report later concluded it was deliberate arson. The JIT found: (1) The factory had refused to pay extortion (bhatta) to a politically-connected network. (2) The fire was started at multiple locations simultaneously — a pattern inconsistent with accidental fire. (3) The factory's emergency exits were found locked from outside — workers died banging on doors they could not open from inside. (4) The factory's fire safety certification had been obtained fraudulently despite substandard conditions.

JIT arrested individuals with alleged connections to MQM's industrial extortion network. The arrests triggered immediate political pressure. The case proceeded through Pakistan's courts for years — with delays, appeals, challenges, and political interference documented by lawyers involved in the case.

The JIT report — a government document — named MQM-connected individuals as alleged architects of the arson. This was a government's own investigation accusing a political party's network of killing 289 workers for refusing to pay extortion. The case then sat in courts for 13 years with no final conviction. This is not a theory about Pakistan's justice system. This is a documented case study of how it works.

The criminal trial began in 2013. As of 2025, it remains unresolved after 13 years. Key factors in the delay: (1) Multiple accused have died of natural causes before verdict. (2) Repeated adjournments. (3) Political pressure documented by defense lawyers. (4) Changes in government that shifted political will to prosecute. (5) Witnesses experiencing threats and intimidation.

The German legal proceedings were more consequential: German retailer KiK, whose supply chain included Ali Enterprises, was sued in German courts by four Pakistani survivors and families. After years of proceedings, KiK agreed to a €5.1 million payment — the first time a European company faced financial accountability for a Pakistani supply chain disaster. A German court provided more justice for Baldia fire victims than Pakistani courts did.

The International Labour Organization (ILO) used the Baldia fire as a case study in global supply chain accountability. It contributed to new EU supply chain due diligence regulations. Globally, the fire changed corporate accountability law. In Pakistan: no factory safety reforms of significance followed.

Karachi has 6,000+ registered industrial units in SITE, Korangi, and KITE. An unknown number of additional unregistered workshops. The fire safety conditions documented in Ali Enterprises in 2012 — locked exits, no sprinklers, overcrowding, fraudulent certifications — exist in hundreds of Karachi factories today. Pakistan's Industrial Relations Act and Factories Act require fire safety compliance. The agencies responsible for enforcing these laws (Sindh Labour Department, SITE administration) conduct inspections that can be resolved with "facilitation fees" to the inspectors.

If you work in a factory: know your emergency exits. If they are locked or blocked: this is a legal violation. You can report to the Sindh Labour Department (0800-70001). The Sindh Labour Department has an official workers' complaints mechanism. Whether it responds effectively is uncertain — but documentation of a complaint creates a legal record that can be used in subsequent legal proceedings.

The families of 289 victims received no compensation from the Pakistani government or factory owners through Pakistani courts. Some received a small payment from a private settlement facilitated by international pressure. Pakistan has no workers' compensation insurance system for industrial accidents. If a worker dies in a factory today, their family's only legal recourse is a civil suit — which takes 10-20 years in Pakistan's court backlog.

The Hidden Crises

Groundwater disappearing. K-Electric charging you for other provinces' electricity. Children vanishing. Port corruption adding 30% to every import. Ghost teachers. Five crises that exist alongside the visible ones.

💧 Crisis 1 — The Water Crisis Behind The Water Crisis
Source: PCRWR Studies, IWA Research 2023

Karachi's Groundwater Table Is Dropping ~1 Metre Per Year

Pakistan's Council of Research in Water Resources (PCRWR) has documented that Karachi's groundwater table is declining approximately 1 metre per year in severely affected areas, due to over-extraction through private tubewells and the city's failure to recharge aquifers. When surface water (KWSB supply) is unavailable or unaffordable, hundreds of thousands of households extract groundwater through private pumps. There is no regulation of this extraction. When the aquifer runs out — which is a matter of when, not if, at current extraction rates — no K-IV project will substitute for it. K-IV provides surface water. Groundwater depletion is a separate, slower, less visible crisis that will hit Karachi's poorest residents first — because they depend most heavily on private wells. No government has commissioned a comprehensive groundwater mapping study for Karachi. This crisis is advancing unseen.

⚡ Crisis 2 — You Pay For Other Provinces' Electricity
Source: NEPRA Tariff Determinations, Dawn Energy Desk Reporting

The "DSS Surcharge" — Karachi Subsidises Rural Pakistan's Power Grid

Your K-Electric bill includes a "Development Surcharge" (DSS or similar levy) that goes to the national power pool — partly funding rural electrification across Pakistan. Karachi — which generates 55% of national taxes — also subsidises electricity infrastructure in other provinces through its power bills, while its own K-Electric grid deteriorates and maintains distribution losses of ~19%. This is a structural cross-subsidy where Pakistan's most economically productive city funds power infrastructure elsewhere while receiving the country's worst urban electricity service. The precise amount of this cross-subsidy has never been clearly communicated to Karachi bill-payers. It appears as line items on electricity bills that most consumers don't examine. You are paying for other people's power while yours is cut 6-10 hours a day.

👶 Crisis 3 — Karachi's Missing Children
Source: CPLC Annual Reports, Edhi Foundation Records

Thousands of Children Disappear in Karachi Every Year. No Central Database Exists.

CPLC (Citizens-Police Liaison Committee) data shows thousands of missing persons reports annually in Karachi, with children representing a significant proportion. The Edhi Foundation's mortuaries — which handle unidentified bodies across Pakistan — hold the remains of unidentified children who were never claimed. Pakistan has no national missing children database — unlike India's TrackChild system or the US National Center for Missing and Exploited Children. When a child goes missing in Karachi: (1) police may or may not file an FIR (they often categorise it as "ran away"); (2) there is no coordinated search protocol; (3) there is no public alert system (no equivalent of the US Amber Alert). Most missing children in Karachi are runaways from domestic abuse situations. A significant number are suspected trafficking victims — Pakistan is listed by the US State Department as a Tier 2 Watch List country for human trafficking. This crisis is invisible in policy because the children it affects have no political voice.

⚓ Crisis 4 — The Port Tax You Pay On Everything
Source: World Bank Doing Business Report, FBR Customs Data, Business Recorder Investigations

How KPT Customs Corruption Adds 15-30% to Every Import — Including Your Medicine and Phone

Pakistan's customs clearance system at Karachi Port Trust (KPT) operates on two tracks: the official "Green Channel" (automated clearance for low-risk importers) and the "Red Channel" (physical inspection). In practice, importers learn quickly that the system has a third track: "facilitation fees" (bribes) that determine whether your cargo clears in hours or weeks. Standard practice documented in multiple business surveys: importers pay 2-5% of cargo value in unofficial fees to customs officials for expedited clearance. For a shipment worth $100,000: $2,000-5,000 in bribes. This cost is passed to consumers. Pakistan's average customs clearance time is 5-7 days — Singapore's is 24 hours. Chapter Twenty-Three — The Full Water Story

Water — The Complete Crisis

The source is polluted. The river is dying. The rain disappears. Three deeper dimensions of Karachi's water catastrophe that go beyond pipes and tankers.

Crisis Layer 1 — Source: PCRWR Studies, Environmental Reports

Keenjhar Lake Is Contaminated — The Water Crisis Starts Before the Pipes

Karachi's primary water source is Keenjhar Lake (also known as Kalri Lake) — a freshwater lake in Sindh, approximately 100km from Karachi. It is one of Asia's largest freshwater lakes and supplies the city via the Hub Dam and Keenjhar supply scheme. But Keenjhar itself has a growing contamination problem: agricultural runoff from surrounding farmland carrying fertilisers and pesticides; industrial effluent from nearby small industries; sewage from the surrounding communities that depend on the lake. Pakistan's PCRWR (Pakistan Council of Research in Water Resources) has documented rising levels of pollutants in Keenjhar water. KWSB's treatment capacity is designed for moderately clean water — not for increasingly contaminated source water. The result: even the water that successfully travels through Karachi's pipes (bypassing the 42% non-revenue water loss) is of decreasing quality at source. The water crisis narrative focuses on supply. The contamination of the supply itself is almost never discussed.

Crisis Layer 2 — Source: WWF Pakistan, IUCN, IRSA Data

The Indus Delta Is Dying — And It's Taking Karachi's Coastal Protection With It

The Indus River — one of the world's great rivers — used to pour approximately 150 million acre-feet of water annually into the Arabian Sea near Karachi. Today it delivers less than 10 million acre-feet — a 93% reduction. The cause: upstream dams (Tarbela, Mangla, and hundreds of barrages) capturing virtually all water for irrigation before it reaches the sea. The consequences for Karachi: Mangrove forests that lined the Karachi coastline are dying — mangroves need the sediment and freshwater flow of the Indus delta. IUCN documents that the Indus delta mangroves have shrunk from 600,000 hectares to less than 160,000 hectares. These mangroves are Karachi's natural coastal protection against storm surges and sea level rise. They are a spawning ground for the fish that feed millions. They are a carbon sink. And they are dying because Pakistan's water management has prioritised upstream agriculture over the entire coastal ecosystem. As sea levels rise and the mangrove buffer disappears, coastal Karachi — including the port area — faces increasing flooding risk that no K-IV project addresses.

Crisis Layer 3 — Source: Urban Resource Centre, Rainfall Data

Karachi Gets 200mm of Rain Annually — And Captures Almost Zero of It

Karachi receives approximately 196-200mm of rainfall annually — concentrated in the July-August monsoon season. For a city of 20 million people, this represents an enormous potential water resource. A simple calculation: 200mm over Karachi's urban area of ~3,500 km² = approximately 700 billion litres of water per year falling on the city. Karachi's annual water demand is approximately 160 billion litres. The city receives more than 4× its annual water needs from rainfall alone — and captures effectively zero of it. No rooftop harvesting systems. No percolation pits. No stormwater retention. The rain that falls on Karachi either floods the streets (because drainage doesn't exist) or runs into the sea (contaminated by garbage and sewage). The same water that kills people through flooding could, with infrastructure investment, significantly reduce the water shortage. Singapore — which receives similar annual rainfall — has built a comprehensive water collection system that provides 30% of national demand from harvested rainwater. Karachi does the opposite: it lets the water drown its people and then asks where the water went.

Chapter Twenty-Six — The System Nobody Explains

Surviving the Bureaucracy

How to actually get a birth certificate. How NADRA works. How courts work. How to register a business. The real processes — including the unofficial fees nobody publishes.

A birth certificate in Pakistan is theoretically free and easy. In practice:

Step 1: Within 60 days of birth, the parent must report to the Union Council of the area where the child was born. Bring: the hospital's birth slip (if hospital birth), both parents' original CNICs, nikah nama (marriage certificate).

Step 2: The UC secretary fills in a Form B (birth registration form). Fee: officially free. Unofficial expectation: ₨200-500 "processing fee."

Step 3: If more than 60 days have passed: late registration requires an affidavit from two witnesses attesting to the birth, plus a sworn statement from the parent. Requires a notary (₨500-1,500).

Step 4: For hospital births in private hospitals: the hospital itself may not have reported to the UC (many don't). Get a birth slip from the hospital records department. Some hospitals charge for this.

Step 5: Take the registered birth certificate to NADRA. This is used to create a Form B (Child Registration Certificate) — the document children need until they get a CNIC at 18.

Why millions don't complete this: Home births (common in poor areas) leave no hospital documentation. Parents without CNICs cannot register a child. Parents from conservative families (where the mother may not have a CNIC) cannot register. Families in katchi abadis without formal addresses have difficulty establishing "place of residence." Estimated 10+ million Pakistanis lack a CNIC — many because their birth was never formally registered. Without a CNIC: cannot vote, cannot have a bank account, cannot register a SIM card, cannot get a passport, cannot formally own property, cannot access most government services.

The UNICEF-supported National Programme for Family Planning and Primary Healthcare has documented that birth registration rates in Karachi's katchi abadis are significantly below the city average. The bureaucratic complexity of registration disproportionately affects the families who need documentation most.

NADRA (National Database and Registration Authority) manages Pakistan's identity system. Understanding it protects you.

Getting a first CNIC (age 18+): Visit a NADRA office with: Form B (child registration certificate), parents' CNICs, B-form. Biometric registration (fingerprints, photo). Fee: approximately ₨750 (normal) or ₨1,500 (urgent). Processing: 10-15 days normal, 3-5 days urgent. Online tracking available.

Why 10+ million Pakistanis still don't have CNICs: (1) Women in conservative families who have never left home and have no school records or birth certificates. (2) Refugees (Afghan, Bengali) who cannot prove Pakistani citizenship. (3) Transgender persons who face registration barriers. (4) People from remote areas where NADRA offices don't exist. (5) People whose parents lacked CNICs and therefore couldn't register them as children.

NADRA data security: The 2024 breach (2.7 million records exposed) revealed that NADRA's internal access controls were inadequate. Your biometric data is stored in NADRA's central database. Pakistan has no comprehensive data protection law. Your fingerprints, iris scans, and personal information are there permanently — with inadequate legal protection.

Useful NADRA services you may not know about: Online CNIC verification (nadra.gov.pk/cnic-status). Overseas Pakistanis can get NICOP (National Identity Card for Overseas Pakistanis) at Pakistani embassies. Digital birth certificates are available through NADRA's online portal without visiting an office.

Pakistan's judicial system has three main tiers relevant to Karachi: Civil Courts / Sessions Courts (first instance for civil and criminal cases), Sindh High Court (appeals, constitutional matters), Supreme Court of Pakistan (final appeal).

The realistic timeline: A simple civil case (debt recovery, property dispute) in a Karachi lower court: 5-15 years from filing to verdict. A criminal case: 3-20 years. Pakistan has 2 million+ pending cases. The Sindh High Court has a backlog of hundreds of thousands of cases. There are approximately 2,500 judges for 220 million people (India has 20,000 for 1.4 billion — also severely under-resourced).

The real costs: Lawyer fees: ₨5,000-20,000 per hearing for a basic lawyer. ₨50,000-500,000+ per hearing for senior lawyers. Each court date is a separate fee. With cases lasting 5-15 years and multiple hearings per year: legal fees easily reach ₨500,000-5,000,000+ for a medium-complexity case. This is why courts are effectively available only to the wealthy.

The alternatives that actually work: Alternate Dispute Resolution (ADR) centres — faster, cheaper, legally binding for civil matters. Karachi ADR Centre (karachiadr.org). Family courts — specifically for family disputes, faster than civil courts. District Consumer Protection Council — for consumer complaints against businesses. These exist. They are underused because nobody teaches citizens they exist.

For property disputes specifically: Karachi's Land Revenue courts are separate from civil courts. If you have a property dispute with KDA, SBCA, or KWSB: an RTI request to the relevant agency's RTI officer is often faster and cheaper than court, and creates an administrative record that strengthens any subsequent legal case.

Pakistan's World Bank Doing Business ranking for "starting a business" is consistently poor. Here is the actual process in Karachi:

1. Choose business structure: Sole proprietorship (simplest), Partnership, Private Limited Company (Pvt Ltd). Most small businesses use sole proprietorship.

2. For Pvt Ltd: Register with SECP (Securities and Exchange Commission of Pakistan) online at eservices.secp.gov.pk. Fee: ₨1,000-5,000. Time: 1-3 days if all documents correct.

3. NTN (National Tax Number): Register with FBR (Federal Board of Revenue) at iris.fbr.gov.pk. Free. Time: 1-3 days. Required for all businesses.

4. Sales Tax Registration (if annual turnover >₨10M): FBR online. Required to charge GST and claim input tax credit.

5. Professional Tax Registration: Sindh Revenue Board for Sindh businesses. Annual fee: ₨10,000-100,000 depending on business type.

6. Trade Licence from KMC: Required for operating in Karachi. Apply at your DMC (District Municipal Corporation). Fee: ₨2,000-20,000. Time: 1-4 weeks. Unofficial expectation at some offices: "facilitation."

7. EOBI Registration (Employees' Old-Age Benefits Institution) if employing >5 people. Social security registration. Required by law, often ignored.

8. SESSI Registration (Sindh Employees' Social Security Institution) if employing >5 people in Sindh.

9. Fire NOC from Karachi Fire Department for any commercial premises.

10. Building use approval from SBCA if converting residential to commercial use.

11. Food Authority licence (PSQCA/PSHA) if food business.

12. DRAP registration if pharmaceutical.

13. Bank account: Requires NTN + SECP registration + utility bill + full KYC. Takes 1-4 weeks at most banks.

14. CCTV registration with Sindh Safe Cities Authority for some commercial areas.

The entire process takes 2-8 weeks minimum with no complications and ₨30,000-200,000 in official and unofficial fees. This is why the informal economy is rational for small businesses: registering formally costs money, creates tax obligations, and brings regulatory scrutiny — without providing any tangible benefit to a small business in Karachi that the government will never actually serve anyway.

Chapter Twenty-Seven — Money Systems Nobody Explains

The Hidden Money Systems

Hawala moves billions outside the banking system. Circular debt has hit Rs 2.6 trillion. The FATF grey list cost Pakistan billions. The Steel Mill loses money every day for 17 years. All connected. None explained publicly.

Hawala (Arabic: trust) is an informal money transfer system that operates entirely outside banks. How it works: you give a hawala broker in Karachi Rs 100,000 and tell him you want it delivered to Dubai. He calls his counterpart in Dubai and tells them to pay equivalent dirhams to your recipient there. The two brokers settle their accounts later through goods, services, or periodic cash transfers. No money crosses a border. No bank is involved. No record exists.

Scale: Pakistan's State Bank estimates hawala transactions at $5-7 billion annually — but this is almost certainly a significant underestimate. World Bank studies suggest Pakistan's informal money transfer flows may equal or exceed its formal remittance system.

Who uses it: (1) Overseas Pakistanis sending money home at better rates than banks. (2) Businesses moving money across borders without tax documentation. (3) Drug traffickers and criminals moving proceeds. (4) Tax evaders moving money offshore. (5) Ordinary families with a relative in a country without formal Pakistani banking access.

Why the government can't stop it: It requires no infrastructure — just trust between two brokers who know each other. It leaves no paper trail. It is embedded in trading and business networks that predate Pakistan. Hawala brokers have operated in South Asia for 1,000+ years. Every crackdown simply pushes it further underground while failing to eliminate it.

The FATF grey listing of Pakistan (2018-2022) was partly a response to hawala's use by terrorist financing networks. Being grey-listed cost Pakistani businesses an estimated $10-38B in increased banking costs, reduced FDI, and restricted access to international financial systems. Karachi's trade finance was directly affected — Pakistani businesses paid higher interest rates and faced longer clearing times for international transactions.

Pakistan's power sector circular debt is one of the most consequential economic problems in the country — and almost no one can explain it clearly. Here it is:

The chain: (1) Power plants (generators) produce electricity and are owed money by DISCOs (distribution companies like LESCO, PESCO, K-Electric). (2) DISCOs are owed money by consumers and the government (subsidised tariffs mean consumers pay less than cost). (3) The government is owed money by DISCOs (through taxes and loans). (4) Nobody pays on time. Nobody pays in full. The unpaid amounts accumulate as "circular debt."

How it reached Rs 2.6 trillion: CPEC power plants were built with guaranteed capacity payments — Pakistan pays whether or not the plant generates power. This added fixed costs that the system cannot afford. Electricity theft at distribution level (officially 20-25% of distributed power is stolen or unbilled). Government subsidies that were promised but not funded. Low billing and collection efficiency in certain DISCOs.

What it means for Karachi: K-Electric (Karachi's utility) is both a victim and contributor to circular debt. It is owed money by NTDC (national grid) and owes money to fuel suppliers. The debt impairs its ability to invest in the distribution upgrades that would reduce its 19% losses. Karachi's industrial sector pays the highest electricity tariffs in Pakistan partly to service the debt structure created by national power sector mismanagement.

The IMF's 2025 programme requires Pakistan to reduce circular debt. Pakistan has agreed to reduce circular debt in 4 previous IMF programmes. Circular debt has grown in each of those programmes. The reason it grows: the political cost of raising electricity tariffs to cost-recovery levels is higher than the cost of continuing to borrow. This pattern will continue until it produces a systemic collapse.

Pakistan Steel Mills (PSM) in Karachi is a case study in how political economy defeats economic rationality. It was built in the 1970s with Soviet assistance as a flagship state industrial project. Peak employment: 22,000 workers. It has been effectively non-operational since 2015.

The numbers: Annual losses accumulated since 2008: Rs 200+ billion. Employees on payroll in 2024 receiving salaries for not working: approximately 9,000-10,000. Steel production: near zero. Privatisation attempts: 5 failed attempts since 2006 (2006, 2012, 2016, 2019, 2021).

Why it's never resolved: PSM employs workers from specific constituencies in Karachi. The unions are politically connected (historically to PPP in Sindh). Shutting PSM means: mass unemployment creating a political crisis, union resistance with potential for violent protest, and the need to pay terminal benefits estimated at Rs 30-50B — money the government doesn't have. Privatising PSM means finding a buyer willing to take on its debt, its land (which is valuable), its workers, and its environmental liabilities (the site is contaminated with decades of industrial waste). Every government has chosen to keep PSM on life support rather than make the painful decision. Every year of delay adds more losses to the public balance sheet. The land PSM sits on, however, is estimated to be worth tens of billions of rupees — which means there are powerful interests who prefer PSM to remain dormant and non-operational rather than be sold, because a sold PSM means the land is monetised by whoever buys it rather than held in a state of expensive limbo.

Pakistan International Airlines (PIA) was founded in Karachi, operates from Karachi, and is headquartered in Karachi. It set the 1962 London speed record. It trained the founders of Emirates and Singapore Airlines. Today:

Annual losses: Rs 50-100B+ per year. Total accumulated debt: Rs 600B+. Fleet age: average 15+ years (industry standard: 8-10 years). On-time performance: consistently near bottom globally. The five privatisation attempts: 2006 (abandoned), 2012 (abandoned), 2016 (partial — 26% stake sold, reversed), 2019 (tenders issued, no qualified bidders), 2021 (abandoned during COVID). 2024: Government announced intent to privatise again. Status: pending.

Why it's never privatised: PIA employs 14,000+ people. It has politically connected unions. Any bidder inherits massive debt. Foreign airlines don't want PIA's aircraft or routes — they want PIA's valuable slots at international airports (London Heathrow, New York JFK etc), which have strict rules about what can be transferred in a privatisation. The airline that once advised Emirates has become a symbol of what happens when a state enterprise is used as an employment programme, a political patronage machine, and a procurement opportunity rather than as a business. It costs Pakistan approximately ₨200+ per passenger per flight in subsidy — paid by taxpayers who often can't afford to fly.

Global Strategic Importance

🌊 Why a Karachi Port Closure Would Affect Global Trade

The Arabian Sea's northern coastline — where Karachi sits — is one of the world's most strategically important shipping lanes. For China specifically: CPEC gives China's western provinces (Xinjiang, Qinghai) access to the Arabian Sea 4,000km shorter than the current Malacca Strait route. Karachi port is not just Pakistan's port — it is China's strategic insurance against a Malacca blockade. This is why China is invested in Karachi's stability in ways that go beyond CPEC's published economics.

The Invisible Economic Relationship

🇦🇫 Karachi as Afghanistan's Commercial Capital

Afghanistan is landlocked. Its nearest deep-water port is Karachi — connected by road through the Khyber Pass (Pakistan) or the Chaman border crossing. 75-80% of Afghanistan's trade passes through Pakistan, primarily via Karachi Port. Afghan traders have had a permanent, documented presence in Karachi's wholesale markets (Jodia Bazaar, Shershah) for generations. This relationship is worth billions in both directions: Pakistan earns transit fees and service revenues; Afghanistan gets access to global markets. Post-Taliban (2021): the relationship has become more complicated as international sanctions affect Afghan banking and trade, but Karachi remains Afghanistan's commercial lifeline. 🇦🇪 The Dubai-Karachi Connection

An estimated 1.2-1.5 million Pakistanis live in Dubai — one of the UAE's largest expatriate communities. Most maintain strong ties to Karachi (where most came from). What the Science Says

🌊 Karachi in Climate Models — 2050 and 2100

Pakistan is ranked 5th most vulnerable to climate change globally (Germanwatch Climate Risk Index). For Karachi specifically: Sea level rise: Conservative models (IPCC) project 0.3-0.5m rise by 2100. Aggressive models: 1-2m. Low-lying coastal areas — the port area, Machar Colony (home to ~500,000 people), Manora Island, Ibrahim Hyderi — face inundation risk. Karachi faces compound climate risk that its current governance structure is completely unprepared to manage.

The Land System

Fard. Intekal. Mutation. Registry. The four documents you need — and why none of them guarantee you actually own what you paid for.

📋 The Four Documents — What They Actually Mean
Document 1

Fard — The Land Record Extract

An official extract from the Sindh Board of Revenue land records. Shows: who the record names as owner, land classification, boundaries. A fard is a snapshot of what records show — NOT a guarantee of actual ownership. Records can be wrong, outdated, or fraudulently altered. Get one at sbr.gos.pk. Cost: few hundred rupees officially, more in practice.

Document 2

Intekal — The Transfer Record

The process of recording ownership transfer in revenue records. Buyer and seller appear before the Patwari (local land record officer). Creates a chain of title. Problem: Patwaris can be bribed to alter intekal records. False mutations are primary fraud mechanisms. The Sindh "digitisation" of land records has been announced multiple times since 2010 — actual coverage remains incomplete and contested.

Document 3

Registry — The Stamp Paper Record

A sale deed registered at the Sub-Registrar's office. Legally stronger than intekal alone. Still not bulletproof: fraudulent documents can be processed with false CNICs (the NADRA breach made fake IDs more accessible). A registry of the same land can exist in two names if corruption allows duplicate registration. Has happened. Courts are full of such cases.

Document 4

Complete Title Chain — Theoretical

Requires: fard + complete intekal chain + all registered sale deeds + no encumbrances. In practice: most Karachi properties have incomplete title chains. Colonial records never digitised. Partition migration created ownership gaps. KDA schemes have unresolved intekal on thousands of plots. A property lawyer reviewing any Karachi property will almost always find at least one gap or irregularity. This uncertainty is a feature — it creates endless litigation and fees for lawyers, officials, and fraudsters.

🌳 The 30% Amenity Plot Scandal
Source: KDA Regulations 2002, Urban Resource Centre Research

Every Karachi Housing Scheme Must Reserve 30% for Public Use. Almost None Do.

Under KDA regulations, every housing scheme must reserve 30% of total land for: parks (minimum 7%), roads, schools, mosques, commercial areas. Hard legal requirement. Urban Resource Centre research covering hundreds of Karachi schemes: the majority either did not reserve the full 30% or reserved land was subsequently converted to residential/commercial use through amended permissions. Three patterns: (1) Developer sells the "park" land as residential plots after getting initial NOC. (2) KDA approves plan amendments reclassifying park land. (3) Politically connected individuals encroach on amenity land and KDA does not act. The 30% represents thousands of acres of public land that should have become Karachi's parks, schools, and mosques — that became instead private profit. This is why Karachi has almost no green space per person compared to any comparable city: the green space was legally required and was stolen.

How Elections Actually Work

The polling agent system. Where results can be manipulated. How Sindh's one-party legislature works. The SRO tax break system. The complete political machine from street to parliament.

🗳️ Election Day — How It Actually Works
1

The Polling Agent System — Your Most Powerful Tool

Each party appoints polling agents inside every polling booth. Their role: verify voter identity, observe counting, sign Form-45 (individual booth results). A well-organised party like PPP in rural Sindh has an agent at every booth. A poorly organised party covers 30-40%. A booth without your party's polling agent can have its Form-45 altered at the counting stage without a witness. This is the most exploited vulnerability in Pakistan's electoral system — and the most preventable by any citizen who registers as a polling agent for their chosen party.

2

Form-45 vs Form-46 — Where the Gap Happens

After counting: each booth produces Form-45 (booth-level results). These are consolidated into Form-46 (constituency results) at the Returning Officer's office. The consolidation gap is the primary opportunity for manipulation. In the 2024 elections, the internet shutdown on election night disrupted independent parallel tabulation while official consolidation continued. ECP's published results showed significant discrepancies from Form-45 totals in multiple Karachi constituencies. Photographing Form-45 at the booth before it leaves is irrefutable evidence. This is why some parties want you not to have that copy.

3

The Presiding Officer's Structural Conflict

The Presiding Officer running each polling booth is a borrowed government servant — usually a school teacher or mid-level official. They receive instructions from District Returning Officers who are provincial government employees. In Sindh, provincial employees answer to PPP. The neutrality of presiding officers in Sindh elections is structurally compromised by this reporting chain. This is not conspiracy theory — it is the designed architecture of the system, and it has been documented in every election observer report for 20 years.

4

How YOU Can Be a Polling Agent

Any voter can register as a polling agent for any registered party. Process: get authorised by your party's constituency candidate → present authorisation letter at polling station on election day → you have the legal right to observe counting and obtain a signed Form-45 copy. A citizen who photographs Form-45 at their booth has irrefutable documentary evidence of that booth's results. This is civic action that directly limits result manipulation — costs nothing and requires no political connections.

📜 How a Bill Becomes Law in Sindh — Why Opposition Votes Are Irrelevant
Source: Sindh Assembly Composition, 18th Amendment

PPP Has an Absolute Majority Alone — The Assembly Is Unicameral With No Effective Check

Sindh Assembly: 130 general seats + 29 women reserved + 9 non-Muslim = 168 seats. PPP holds over 100 general seats — absolute majority alone, before reserved seat allocation. Reserved seats allocated proportionally give PPP additional seats. Practical consequence: no legislation passes without PPP consent, and any legislation PPP wants passes regardless of what opposition does. MQM-P, JI, PTI, independent candidates can speak, raise objections, walk out. The vote count is predetermined. The Sindh Assembly operates as a one-party legislature with ceremonial opposition. The only checks on PPP Sindh are: the federal government (when it has opposing interests), the Superior Courts (with their own politicisation issues), or organised public pressure at a scale that makes inaction politically more costly than reform. None of these checks is currently operating effectively on Karachi's behalf.

⚙️ SROs — How Pakistan's Government Gives Tax Breaks Without Parliament
Source: FBR SRO Database, Dawn Business Desk, IMF GCDA 2025

Thousands of Active SROs Grant Tax Exemptions to Politically Connected Industries — Zero Parliamentary Vote Required

A Statutory Regulatory Order (SRO) is issued by a ministry or FBR with the force of law, bypassing parliamentary debate. SROs can: grant tax exemptions, modify customs duties, waive penalties, change regulatory requirements. Permitted under enabling legislation. In practice: used for decades to grant massive tax breaks to politically connected businesses that would never survive parliamentary scrutiny. Documented examples: SROs exempting specific textile exporter categories (benefiting large export houses with political connections); SROs reducing duties on specific imported goods (benefiting connected importers); SROs waiving penalties on large FBR defaulters. The IMF's November 2025 GCDA report identified the SRO system as a core mechanism of Pakistan's "elite capture" problem. Pakistan's largest companies have built their profitability partly on SRO-granted exemptions their competitors cannot access. This is the legal architecture of the inequality the IMF diagnosed — and it operates entirely within Pakistani law.

🏛️ Actually Registering a Political Party — Step by Step

Under the Political Parties Order 2002 (as amended), to register a political party with the Election Commission of Pakistan:

Step 1 — Hold a founding convention: Organise a convention of party members. The ECP requires evidence of organisational activity and membership. Technically there is no strict minimum member number specified, but you need enough to demonstrate a functioning organisation.

Step 2 — Draft a party constitution: Must include: party name and symbol, objectives, membership rules, internal election procedures, and financial management rules. A lawyer with political party experience can draft this for ₨15,000-30,000. Alternatively, study existing party constitutions (publicly available on ECP website) and adapt them.

Step 3 — Open a bank account: The party must have a dedicated bank account. All income and expenditure must be documented. The ECP requires annual financial statements.

Step 4 — Submit registration application to ECP: Forms available at ecp.gov.pk. Include: party constitution, list of founding members with CNICs, party flag/symbol, bank account details. ECP reviews and may request additional information.

Step 5 — Annual compliance: File annual membership and financial statements with ECP. Failure to comply leads to deregistration (which is how many small parties disappear).

Starting as a registered civil society organisation (under Societies Registration Act 1860 or Voluntary Social Welfare Agencies Ordinance 1961) is easier, faster, and lets you do almost all civic work — documentation, advocacy, community organising, RTI filing — without the full burden of party compliance. Many successful political movements in other countries started as NGOs before formalising as parties.

🕵️ The CIA in Karachi — 1950s to 1980s
Source: Declassified CIA documents (FOIA releases), Steve Coll "Ghost Wars" 2004, Lawrence Wright "The Looming Tower" 2006

Karachi Was One of the CIA's Most Important Cold War Stations — Documented in Declassified Files

From the early 1950s, Karachi served as a critical CIA forward operating base for operations against the Soviet Union. The documented activities:

1950s — The U-2 programme: CIA U-2 spy planes conducting surveillance missions over Soviet territory operated from Pakistani air bases including Karachi's Mauripur (now PAF Base Faisal). The 1960 U-2 incident — when pilot Gary Powers was shot down over the USSR — originated from Peshawar's Badaber base, but Karachi was the logistics hub. Pakistan received substantial military and economic aid in exchange for this access. The Pakistani public was told nothing. Source: Declassified CIA operational records, Christopher Andrew "The Sword and the Shield."

1950s-70s — SAVAK in Karachi: During the Shah of Iran's era, the Iranian intelligence service SAVAK maintained active offices in Karachi. Iran and Pakistan cooperated closely through CENTO (Central Treaty Organisation — a US-backed anti-Soviet alliance). SAVAK officers operated in Karachi monitoring Iranian dissidents and leftist movements among Karachi's population. When the Shah fell in 1979, these networks collapsed — and some of their infrastructure was absorbed by Pakistani intelligence. Source: Ervand Abrahamian "A History of Modern Iran," academic intelligence histories.

1979-1989 — The Afghan Jihad pipeline through Karachi: After the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan, the CIA worked with the ISI to channel weapons, money, and fighters to the Afghan Mujahideen. Karachi was the primary entry point for this material. The CIA station in Karachi was one of the largest in the world during this period. CIA Director William Casey personally visited Karachi multiple times to supervise the operation. At peak operation, the CIA was moving approximately $2 billion per year through Pakistan — much of it through Karachi's port and financial system. The Blowback: The same infrastructure that moved CIA weapons into Afghanistan also moved drugs out. The same networks that trained and armed Mujahideen fighters created the armed, radicalised, weapon-saturated environment that produced Karachi's ethnic violence of the 1990s. The CIA operation that was supposed to defeat Soviet communism created the conditions for 1,742 people killed in Karachi in 1995 alone. This is not conspiracy theory. It is documented history acknowledged by CIA analysts themselves.

🚂 The 1947-1953 Refugee Crisis Nobody Talks About
Source: Government of Pakistan census records, Vazira Zamindar "The Long Partition" 2007, Ian Talbot "Karachi: A History" 2020

Karachi's Population Exploded from 400,000 to 1.5 Million in 5 Years — and Nobody Had a Plan

In 1947, Karachi had approximately 400,000 residents — a modest port city. By 1951, the population had reached 1.5 million. By 1953, it was approaching 2 million. The cause: the largest forced migration in human history — the partition of British India.

Who came and where they went: Muhajirs (Muslim refugees from India) arrived primarily from: Uttar Pradesh and Bihar (Urdu-speaking educated class who became Pakistan's bureaucratic backbone), Bombay (trading communities — Memonis, Bohras, Khojas who brought commercial networks), and Hyderabad Deccan (after the Indian annexation of Hyderabad in 1948). They arrived with nothing — or with everything they could carry — into a city that had no infrastructure to receive them.

The camps that became permanent: The government established refugee camps throughout Karachi. Camp conditions were catastrophic — overcrowding, disease, inadequate sanitation. Liaquatabad was established as a planned township for refugees. Orangi was initially a refugee settlement. Korangi was developed with World Bank assistance in the 1950s specifically for refugees. What was supposed to be temporary became permanent. The "temporary" refugee settlements of 1947-1953 are now some of Karachi's most densely populated permanent neighbourhoods — 70+ years later, still without adequate infrastructure because they were never meant to last.

The ethnic hierarchy that was created: Muhajir refugees, particularly the educated Urdu-speaking class from UP and Bihar, arrived with cultural capital — literacy, professional skills, bureaucratic connections to the new Pakistani state — that the indigenous Sindhi population largely lacked. They rapidly dominated the civil service, professions, and commerce. This created the ethnic resentment that exploded in the 1972 language riots and has never been resolved. Every ethnic conflict in Karachi since 1947 traces to decisions made in the refugee crisis of 1947-1953.

🏭 Bhutto's Nationalisation (1972-1977) — What It Actually Destroyed
Source: World Bank Pakistan assessments, academic economic histories, State Bank of Pakistan historical records

Nationalisation Destroyed Karachi's Industrial Base — and the Effects Are Still Here

Zulfikar Ali Bhutto's government nationalised major industries between 1972-1974: 31 major industrial units in basic metals, chemicals, and engineering; agro-industries; banks (all major private banks became state-owned); insurance companies; shipping companies; educational institutions. This was the single most economically consequential decision in Karachi's history — and it was a catastrophe.

What happened to industrial productivity: Nationalised industries, run by government appointees rather than owners with equity stakes, deteriorated rapidly. The Pakistan Steel Mills — nationalised and then built with Soviet assistance — became the zombie company it remains today (losing Rs 200B+ while employing thousands who do nothing). The textile mills that were nationalised saw productivity collapse. Private investment in industry collapsed because businesspeople feared further nationalisation.

The banking nationalisation: All private banks became state banks. Political lending replaced commercial lending — loans to politically connected people who never repaid them. By the 1990s, Pakistan's state banks were technically insolvent due to non-performing loans to politically connected borrowers. The culture of political lending created the circular debt and non-performing loan crisis that still plagues Pakistan's banking system.

The 1972 language policy: Simultaneously, Bhutto's government issued the Sindhi Language Authority Act, making Sindhi the official language of Sindh and requiring its use in government. Karachi's Urdu-speaking Muhajir community — who had dominated government employment — felt existentially threatened. The resulting riots killed dozens. This is the direct origin moment of the MQM — the political party that would dominate Karachi for 30 years emerged from the Muhajir community's existential fear created by Bhutto's language policy.

💣 The Zia Years (1977-1988) — How Afghan Jihad Armed Karachi
Source: Steve Coll "Ghost Wars," Zahid Hussain "Frontline Pakistan," Human Rights Watch Pakistan reports

The Specific Numbers: How Weapons Flooded Karachi During the Afghan War

The official account: weapons destined for Afghan Mujahideen were strictly controlled by the ISI and delivered to fighters in Afghanistan. The reality, documented by multiple researchers: a massive percentage leaked into Karachi's criminal market.

The logistics chain: weapons arrived at Karachi Port from Egypt, China, and the US. They were moved to ISI warehouses in Karachi and Rawalpindi. They were transported by truck to the Afghan border. At every stage of this supply chain — port, warehouse, truck — weapons were diverted by corrupt handlers and sold on Karachi's open market. By the mid-1980s, Kalashnikovs were openly available in Karachi's Sohrab Goth market for approximately Rs 3,000-5,000 (equivalent to a month's salary for a middle-class worker). This is documented in journalistic accounts from the period.

The heroin dimension: The CIA operation that funded the Mujahideen also involved turning a blind eye to Mujahideen commanders who financed their operations through opium and heroin. Afghanistan became the world's largest heroin producer during this period. Karachi was the primary export point. A 1986 US State Department report acknowledged that Pakistani officials involved in the Afghan supply chain were simultaneously involved in heroin trafficking — and the CIA did nothing about it because disrupting them would disrupt the weapons supply. Source: Alfred McCoy "The Politics of Heroin" 1991, declassified State Department cables.

The long-term consequence: By 1988, when Zia died, Karachi had: an estimated 1.5 million Kalashnikovs in civilian hands; established heroin distribution networks reaching into every major neighbourhood; battle-hardened ethnic militias connected to both Afghan jihad networks and domestic political parties; and a police force that had been comprehensively corrupted by drug money. The 1,742 people killed in 1995 were killed with weapons whose presence in Karachi traces directly to 1979-1988.

🇦🇫 The Afghan Transit Trade — $4-6 Billion Annually
Source: World Bank Afghan Transit Trade report 2020, UNODC reports, Dawn business desk investigations

Afghanistan Is Landlocked. Everything It Imports Comes Through Karachi. A Large Percentage Never Reaches Afghanistan.

The Afghanistan-Pakistan Transit Trade Agreement (APTTA) allows goods destined for landlocked Afghanistan to enter Pakistan through Karachi Port duty-free and transit to the Afghan border. The legal system works. The illegal overlay on top of it is massive.

The smuggling mechanism: Goods declared for Afghanistan are cleared through Karachi Customs duty-free. They are loaded onto trucks. In theory, Pakistani customs seals the trucks and they are escorted to the border. In practice: a percentage of these trucks never reach Afghanistan. They are diverted — their seals broken — and the goods are sold in Pakistani markets. Electronics declared for Afghanistan are sold in Karachi's Saddar market. Tyres declared for Afghanistan are sold in Lahore. Consumer goods declared for Afghanistan are sold in every major Pakistani city. Pakistan's domestic industry — which pays full import duties — competes against smuggled goods that entered duty-free as "Afghan transit."

The scale: Pakistan Customs estimates "leakage" from Afghan transit at billions of rupees annually. The Federal Board of Revenue has repeatedly flagged the APTTA as a major revenue loss. Textile manufacturers, electronics importers, and consumer goods manufacturers have formally complained to the government that Afghan transit smuggling undercuts them. The government acknowledges the problem. The APTTA continues because disrupting it would disrupt the Pakistan-Afghanistan relationship — which serves other strategic interests.

Source: FBR Customs, ANF seizure records

🥇 Gold Smuggling — Karachi as South Asia's Entry Point

Pakistan's gold import is subject to duties. The price differential between official and unofficial gold creates a persistent smuggling incentive. ANF and Customs document hundreds of gold seizures annually at Karachi airport and port. Methodology: gold is typically carried by passengers on Dubai-Karachi flights (Dubai is a major gold trading hub), hidden in luggage, clothing, or bodily cavities. Commercial-scale smuggling uses cargo containers with concealed gold. Estimated annual smuggled gold entering Pakistan through Karachi: several hundred kilograms to tonnes. The gold market on Karachi's Zarkoon Gali (old city) is one of South Asia's largest and operates in a regulatory grey zone. Much of the gold traded there has no documented origin.

🏠 Benami Property — The Scale of Hidden Ownership

A benami transaction is where property is purchased in someone else's name (a nominee, often a domestic worker, a relative, or a fake identity) to hide the real owner. Pakistan's Benami Transactions (Prohibition) Act 2017 made this illegal and created an enforcement mechanism. FBR's Benami Zone has identified billions of rupees in benami properties since 2017. In Karachi specifically: high-value commercial properties in SITE and Korangi registered in employees' names; residential properties in DHA and Clifton registered in wives' or parents' names while real owners are politically exposed persons; plots in new housing schemes registered in CNICs of people who have no income to explain the purchase. The Benami law has produced some freezing of assets — but prosecutions and actual asset forfeitures remain limited due to court backlogs.

💵 How Hawala Sets Pakistan's Real Exchange Rate

The State Bank Pretends to Set the Exchange Rate. The Hawala Market Actually Does.

How hawala works technically: Someone in the UK wants to send £1,000 to their family in Karachi. They give £1,000 to a hawala dealer in Bradford. The Bradford dealer calls their partner in Karachi. The Karachi partner gives the equivalent in rupees to the recipient family — from rupees they already have. No money physically crosses the border. The two dealers settle their balance periodically through other means (gold, trade invoices, reverse transactions). Transaction: no bank, no SWIFT, no documentation, no forex regulation.

The scale in Karachi: Estimates suggest 30-50% of Pakistan's remittances flow through informal hawala rather than banking channels. State Bank efforts to formalise remittances (the Roshan Digital Account programme) have shifted some flows to official channels — but hawala persists because it is: faster (same-day settlement vs 2-3 days for banks), cheaper (no bank fees), and accessible to recipients without bank accounts.

How it sets the exchange rate: The hawala rate for dollars-to-rupees is set by supply and demand among hawala dealers — particularly concentrated in Karachi's money markets on I.I. Chundrigar Road and the old city. When hawala dealers set a rate that diverges from the official interbank rate, pressure builds. In 2022-23, when the official rupee was artificially held at Rs 220/$ while hawala rates reached Rs 260/$, importers bypassed the official system entirely. The currency crisis of 2022-2023 was partly a crisis of the official rate failing to reflect the hawala reality for too long. Pakistan's foreign exchange crises consistently involve this divergence between official and hawala rates.

Specific Karachi Neighbourhoods at Risk — Based on Elevation and Sea Level Projections

Karachi sits on the Arabian Sea coast. Much of its western and southern areas are at low elevation — 0-3 metres above sea level. Under IPCC's moderate warming scenario (2°C by 2100), sea levels in the Arabian Sea are projected to rise 0.3-0.6 metres by 2100. Under high-emission scenarios: 0.6-1.0 metres or more.

Areas at highest risk by 2050 (1°C scenario):

Keamari and Karachi Port area — elevation 1-2m, directly coastal. Home to the port infrastructure that handles 76% of Pakistan's seaborne trade. A 0.5m sea level rise combined with storm surge during monsoon would regularly inundate this area. Keamari is also home to hundreds of thousands of residents in katchi abadis at sea level.

Parts of Lyari — the Lyari River (which still flows seasonally) combined with sea level rise creates compound flooding risk for low-lying parts of Lyari near the river mouth.

3-5°C Hotter Than Surrounding Areas — Urban Heat Island Effect

Karachi's rapid, unplanned urbanisation has created a massive urban heat island: concrete and asphalt replacing vegetation absorbs heat and radiates it back at night; industrial activity in SITE, Korangi, and Landhi generating direct heat; K-Electric's diesel generators (run by millions of households during load shedding) generating heat across the city; almost no tree cover or green space to provide cooling (Karachi has less than 2 square metres of green space per person — WHO recommends 9 sq metres minimum). Result: Karachi's dense urban core is consistently 3-5°C hotter than the surrounding semi-arid landscape. In a city where temperatures already reach 42-45°C in summer, an additional 3-5°C is the difference between survivable heat and lethal heat.

🌿 Mangroves — 60% Destroyed, Coastal Protection Collapsing

Karachi's Indus Delta mangroves were historically one of the world's largest mangrove ecosystems — covering approximately 600,000 hectares. They served as: coastal storm surge protection (mangroves reduce wave height by 50-70%); nurseries for Karachi's fishing industry (80%+ of commercial fish species spend juvenile phases in mangroves); carbon sequestration; and water filtration. Since 1990: approximately 60% of Karachi's mangroves have been destroyed by industrial effluent, port expansion, urban encroachment, the diversion of Indus River water for irrigation (mangroves need freshwater from the river mixed with seawater to survive), and deliberate clearing for aquaculture ponds. The Aga Khan Foundation and WWF Pakistan have active mangrove restoration programmes — but restoration at the scale needed requires government commitment that does not currently exist.

🌧️ The Monsoon Is Intensifying
Source: Pakistan Meteorological Department records, Climate Change Division Pakistan, IPCC AR6

Karachi's "Modest" Monsoon Is Becoming Catastrophic — 2020, 2022, 2024 Are the New Normal

Karachi historically received limited monsoon rainfall — approximately 170-200mm annually, concentrated in July-August. This modest rainfall was manageable by the city's drainage system (even though that system was inadequate). Climate change is breaking this pattern. Karachi is now experiencing increasingly intense rainfall events — not more rain spread over the season, but the same or more total rain delivered in concentrated extreme events.

2020 monsoon: Karachi received 484mm of rain in August 2020 alone — more than double the annual average in a single month. Flooding killed 40+ people. Entire neighbourhoods were submerged for days. This was called "unprecedented." It was followed by 2022.

2022 monsoon: Part of Pakistan's catastrophic 2022 floods that submerged one-third of the country. Karachi's drainage collapsed entirely in several areas. K-Electric infrastructure failed. The death toll from heat-related illness combined with flooding in the same season illustrated climate change's compound effects.

2024 monsoon: Again, extreme rainfall events overwhelming drainage infrastructure that was designed for the 1960s climate pattern — not the 2020s reality. The infrastructure Pakistan's cities are built on assumes a climate that no longer exists. Karachi's stormwater drainage was designed for rainfall patterns of 40-60 years ago. Climate models project continued intensification of extreme rainfall events in South Asia under all warming scenarios. There is no plan in Karachi to adapt to this reality.

📋 How to File an RTI Request — Step by Step
Sindh Transparency and Right to Information Act 2016 — your constitutional right under Article 19A

The RTI Is Your Most Powerful Civic Tool — Almost Nobody Uses It

The Right to Information Act gives every Pakistani citizen the legal right to request documents, data, and information from any government agency. The agency must respond within 21 days. If they don't, it's an offence. If they refuse without valid reason, you can appeal. It costs nothing. It requires no lawyer. You just need to write a letter.

What you can request: maintenance contracts, development project budgets, tender awards, school attendance records, water supply schedules, salary information of public officials, meeting minutes, audit reports — anything that is not specifically exempted (national security, personal privacy of third parties).

Every government agency in Sindh is required to designate a Public Information Officer (PIO) — the specific person who handles RTI requests. Finding the PIO:

Option A (Easiest): Call the agency's main number and ask "Who is your Public Information Officer for RTI requests under the Sindh Transparency Act 2016?" They are legally required to tell you.

Option B: Check the agency website — PIO contact details should be published (they often aren't, which is itself a violation you can report).

Key agencies and addresses for Karachi RTIs:
KWSB: Water Board Road, Karachi — PIO: Company Secretary
KMC: Municipal Corporation Building, M.A. Jinnah Road
SBCA: Sindh Building Control Authority, Block 3, Clifton
KDA: Karachi Development Authority, Sector 28, Gulshan
Sindh Education Dept: New Secretariat Building, Karachi
K-Electric: KE Tower, Stadium Road (private company but many functions are regulable under NEPRA RTI)

If you don't know which agency is responsible for your issue: file with the Sindh Information Commissioner who will direct it to the right department. SIC contact: sindh.gov.pk/dpt/SIC

Specific example questions that work well:

For water issues: "Please provide: (1) The maintenance contract for water supply pipes in UC [number], area [name], for FY 2023-24 (2) The contractor's name and amount paid (3) The inspection records showing completion of work."

For school issues: "Please provide: (1) The attendance records for all teachers at Government School [name] for October-November 2024 (2) The names and designations of all employees on the school's payroll (3) Any audit findings related to this school in the last 3 years."

How to submit: Hand-deliver the letter to the agency's office and get a receipt stamp on your copy. OR send by registered post (keep the receipt). OR email to the PIO if they have an email — follow up with a hard copy. Always keep a copy of what you sent and proof of delivery.

The 21-day countdown starts from the day they receive it — not from when you wrote it. Your delivery receipt is your evidence.

If they respond within 21 days: Excellent. Review the information. If it's incomplete or they've only partially answered, write back requesting the specific missing information — this starts a new 21-day clock.

If they don't respond in 21 days (First Appeal): Write to the Head of the agency (Secretary/Director General/CEO) — this is the First Appellate Authority. Reference your original request date, state they have not responded, and request immediate compliance. Give them 15 days.

If still no response (Second Appeal to Sindh Information Commissioner):

Sindh Information Commissioner
Email: info@sic.sindh.gov.pk
Address: Room No. 204, New Secretariat Building, Karachi
Online: sindh.gov.pk/dpt/SIC

The SIC can: order the agency to provide the information, impose a fine of Rs 500/day on the PIO for delay (up to Rs 25,000), recommend disciplinary action against the PIO.

Document everything publicly. Post your RTI request on Twitter/X with the agency tagged. Post updates when you file appeals. Public documentation changes the cost calculation for officials — it is much harder to ignore an RTI that 5,000 people have seen than one sitting in a filing cabinet.

These are the highest-impact RTI requests a young Karachite can file. Each one has produced documented results when filed by civic activists:

1. The ghost teacher RTI (to Sindh Education Dept):
"Provide the attendance register for all teaching staff at [School Name] for the months of September, October, November 2024, signed by the headmaster. Also provide the current salary roll for all employees at this school."

2. The water contract RTI (to KWSB):
"Provide all maintenance and repair contracts for water supply infrastructure in Union Council [number], District [name], awarded during FY2023-24 and FY2024-25, including contractor names, amounts, and completion certification."

3. The development budget RTI (to KMC or DMC):
"Provide a list of all development projects approved and funded for Union Council [number] in FY2024-25, with project descriptions, allocated budgets, implementing agencies, and current completion status."

4. The park/amenity RTI (to KDA or DMC):
"Provide the original approved layout plan for [Housing Scheme name] showing designated amenity plots (parks, schools, mosques). Also provide a list of any layout plan amendments approved after original sanction, with dates and approving authorities."

✊ How to Actually Hold Officials Accountable

citizen.gov.pk — or download the "Pakistan Citizen Portal" app. This is the federal government's complaint system. Every complaint gets a case number. There are mandatory response deadlines. You can track your complaint status.

How to file a complaint that actually gets action:

1. Be specific: Not "the road in my area is broken." Instead: "The road on [Street Name], between [Landmark A] and [Landmark B], in UC [number], has been broken since [approximate date]. The pothole is approximately [size]. It caused [specific incident] on [date]."

2. Attach evidence: Photos dated with your phone's camera. Photos showing the problem clearly with a person standing next to it for scale.

3. Select the right department: Roads → NHA (national) or Works & Services (provincial) or DMC (district). Water → KWSB. Electricity → K-Electric or NEPRA. Schools → Sindh Education Department.

4. Share publicly: Post your complaint number on social media. Tag the relevant official accounts. This does not guarantee action but it raises the cost of inaction.

5. Follow up ruthlessly: If the case is closed without resolution, reopen it immediately. File the same complaint again. File an RTI asking about the original complaint. File with the federal ombudsman if the case was at a federal agency.

The Citizen Portal has documented cases of complaints being resolved — particularly for basic infrastructure issues in middle-class areas where officials fear media attention. It works less reliably in low-income areas. This is why organising collectively (10 families filing the same complaint) consistently outperforms individual filings.

UC meetings are legally public. They are supposed to happen regularly (monthly in theory). Your UC councillor is the lowest level of government — and therefore the most accessible and the most directly responsible for your street-level issues.

Finding your UC and councillor: ECP website (ecp.gov.pk) → Voter list → Enter your CNIC → Your UC number and constituency appear. Google "[Your UC number] Karachi councillor" or ask KMC directly.

What happens at a UC meeting: Councillors discuss local issues, development priorities, and budget allocations. The public can attend. The public can raise issues. There is usually a "public representation" portion.

What to do there: Bring written documentation of the issue you're raising. Cite specific laws or budget lines if possible. Ask the specific councillor to sign a written acknowledgment that you raised the issue (they hate this — do it anyway, it creates a record). Bring at least 2-3 other affected residents. Bring a smartphone to record the proceedings (this is legal in public meetings).

What if the councillor doesn't come: This is extremely common. Document the absence. File on Citizen Portal: "UC [number] meeting scheduled for [date] was not held / councillor was absent." If this happens consistently, it is grounds for a formal complaint to the Local Government Department.

Your UC councillor's salary comes from public funds. Their attendance at meetings is a legal obligation. Filing formal complaints about non-attendance creates a documented record — and documented records have consequences for those who want to stand for re-election.

Most people think the High Court is only for the rich. It isn't. The SHC has specific mechanisms for ordinary citizens:

Writ petition under Article 199: Any citizen can file a writ petition in the Sindh High Court for violation of a fundamental right or failure of a government body to perform its legal function. Filing fee: Rs 500-2,000. A lawyer: Rs 10,000-30,000 for a basic writ. The SHC has issued orders against KWSB, KDA, KMC, and other agencies in response to citizen petitions.

What types of cases SHC handles well: Illegal demolitions (injunctions can be granted within hours), government agency ignoring RTI requests, officials not performing mandatory duties, property rights violations.

Free legal help for SHC petitions: Legal Aid Committee of Pakistan Bar Council (Karachi chapter) — provides free or subsidised legal aid. HRCP: 042-35761999. TI Pakistan's ALAC centre for corruption-related cases.

The Supreme Court Article 184(3) option: For issues of national/public importance involving fundamental rights — any citizen can petition directly. No lower court journey required. Used by ordinary citizens to get action on environmental violations, illegal demolitions, fundamental rights breaches.

The most powerful legal tool in Pakistan that almost no ordinary citizen uses: filing a contempt of court application when a government agency violates a court order. Courts take contempt seriously. Officials who have ignored RTI orders, Citizen Portal resolutions, and ombudsman findings have complied when faced with contempt proceedings.

A polling agent is a representative of a candidate or political party who sits inside the polling station on election day to monitor the voting process. This is the single most important anti-fraud role available to ordinary citizens. Without polling agents, fraud is much easier. With informed, determined polling agents, fraud is much harder.

Who can be a polling agent: Any adult Pakistani with a CNIC. You are appointed by a candidate or party. You can approach any political party (or an independent candidate) and volunteer to be their polling agent in your own constituency.

What you do on election day: Sit at the polling station from opening to close. Watch every vote being cast. Watch the count. Receive a copy of Form-45 (the official result form) at your station — this is your most critical task. Your signed Form-45 is the legal record of results at your station. In Pakistan's history, elections have been stolen between the polling station (Form-45) and the final consolidated result (Form-47). If you have a signed Form-45, you have evidence.

What you can object to: A voter whose CNIC doesn't match the list. Impersonation. Booth capturing. The Presiding Officer doing anything irregular. Every objection must be written in the official register — demand this.

In the 2024 election, the largest single source of documented irregularities was the discrepancy between Form-45 (station-level results held by polling agents) and Form-47 (final consolidated results). Polling agents who had Form-45 copies were the primary witnesses in election tribunal cases. Be that person.

🧠 Critical Thinking — The Skill Pakistan's System Never Teaches

Step 1 — Who is saying it and why? Every source has interests. Dawn criticising PTI: what is Dawn's editorial position? ARY defending PTI: who owns ARY? A government press release claiming success: what would they say if it was failing? Don't dismiss — contextualise.

Step 2 — Is there a primary source? "Politician says X" is not information. Ask: is there a court record, audit report, official data, or academic study that establishes X independently? This guide cites primary sources specifically so you can check them.

Step 3 — Correlation is not causation. "Pakistan's economy grew during Nawaz Sharif's tenure" does not mean Nawaz Sharif grew the economy. Global commodity prices, remittances, and the IMF programme also moved the economy. Separating what the government did from what happened during the government's tenure requires knowing both.

Step 4 — What is missing? The most important analytical skill. When FBR announces tax collection records, what is the tax-to-GDP ratio? When a government announces new schools built, what are the school quality metrics? When a politician claims to fight corruption, what do their own assets declarations show? Absence of information is itself information.

Step 5 — Reverse image search everything. Pakistan's social media is saturated with old photos presented as current events, photos from other countries presented as Pakistan, fabricated screenshots. Google Images → drag any photo → see where it originally appeared. Takes 10 seconds. Saves you spreading misinformation.

The single most important news literacy skill for Pakistanis: distinguishing between "a politician said X" and "X is true." Most Pakistani news coverage is he-said-she-said. Almost no political reporting is document-based. Train yourself to always ask: what is the document? What is the data? What is the court record? If there isn't one, treat the claim as allegation, not fact.

GDP (Gross Domestic Product): Total value of everything produced in Pakistan in a year. Pakistan 2025: ~$410 billion. This is the economy's size. It tells you nothing about distribution. GDP per capita (~$1,500) tells you the average — which means almost nothing in a country with extreme inequality. When someone says "Pakistan's economy grew 3%," they mean the total value of production grew 3%. It says nothing about whether your life got 3% better.

Inflation: When money supply grows faster than goods, each rupee buys less. Pakistan's peak inflation 2023: 38%. Something that cost Rs 100 in 2022 cost Rs 138 in 2023. If your salary rose 15% during that period, your real purchasing power fell 23%. This is why Karachi families who "got a raise" still feel poorer.

Exchange rate: Rs/$ rate tells you how many rupees buy one dollar. When the rupee weakens: imports (oil, medicine, machinery) cost more in rupees → inflation rises. Remittances buy more rupees → your relatives abroad send the same dollars but you receive more rupees. Exports become cheaper for foreigners → good for exporters. Pakistan's rupee lost ~50% value in 2022-23, effectively doubling the cost of all imports.

Interest rates: SBP policy rate was 22% in 2023. High rates: borrowing expensive → businesses invest less → economy slows → inflation falls. Low rates: borrowing cheap → investment rises → economy grows → inflation can rise. The SBP was at 22% specifically to fight 38% inflation. It worked — inflation fell to ~12% by 2025.

The circular debt (Rs 2.6 trillion): Power companies owe money to fuel suppliers who owe money to banks who owe money to the government which owes money to power companies. Nobody pays. The debt grows. Your electricity bill includes a surcharge to partially service this debt. You are paying for 30 years of governments subsidising electricity and not paying the full cost.

📈 How the Karachi Stock Exchange Actually Works
Source: PSX annual reports, SECP data, academic research on Pakistan capital markets

The KSE-100 at 174,000+ — Who Is Actually Moving It and How Retail Investors Get Burned

The Pakistan Stock Exchange (PSX) has 535 listed companies and a KSE-100 index that hit record highs in 2025. Who is actually trading?

Market structure: Institutional investors (mutual funds, insurance companies, pension funds, foreign portfolio investors) dominate trading volume. Retail investors — ordinary Pakistanis buying shares — represent a small fraction of trading but are systematically disadvantaged.

How retail investors get burned: (1) Insider trading: Corporate announcements in Pakistan are regularly preceded by abnormal price movements — indicating trading on inside information before public announcement. SECP enforcement is limited. (2) Pump and dump: A coordinated group buys shares of a small company, drives the price up through volume, promotes it on social media and WhatsApp groups ("guaranteed returns"), retail investors buy at the top, the group sells, price collapses. This pattern is documented repeatedly in Pakistan's market. (3) Leverage traps: Brokers offer leveraged trading (margin financing) to retail investors — allowing them to invest 5-10× their capital. When the market drops, losses are multiplied. Retail investors in Pakistan have been wiped out repeatedly by leveraged positions in market corrections.

The families that control the market: Several large family groups (Dawood, Habib, Mansha, Lakhani, Nishat) have interlinked shareholdings across banks, insurance companies, and industrial companies that are all listed on PSX. Their portfolios are so large that their investment decisions themselves move the market. When Mian Muhammad Mansha's Nishat Group moves capital between sectors, it creates price signals that the rest of the market follows. This is legal. It is also structural market power that ordinary investors cannot compete with.

📜 How Sindh Assembly Actually Works — And Inheritance Law

Sindh Assembly sessions: The Sindh Assembly (located at Karachi's Assembly Hall, M.A. Jinnah Road) holds sessions that are public. Gallery access for ordinary citizens is technically permitted — contact the Sindh Assembly secretariat for gallery passes. Sessions are also live-streamed on the Sindh Assembly website (sindh.gov.pk/dpt/Assembly) and on YouTube.

How to submit public input: Technically, citizens can write to the Speaker of the Sindh Assembly requesting that a matter be raised in the Assembly. In practice, this is most effective when: the issue has documented public importance, the letter is signed by multiple citizens or an organised group, and a sympathetic MPA agrees to raise the issue formally. The most effective route for ordinary citizens: contact your constituency MPA directly with documented grievances — their contact information is listed on the Sindh Assembly website. An MPA who receives 50 documented constituent complaints about the same issue is more likely to raise it than one who receives none.

Where to find voting records: The Sindh Assembly website publishes division lists (how MPAs voted on specific motions). Voter awareness organisations like PILDAT (Pakistan Institute of Legislative Development and Transparency) — pildat.org — publish MPA attendance records and legislative performance assessments. These are publicly available and almost never looked at by constituents.

Pakistan's inheritance law is governed by Islamic inheritance rules (for Muslims) codified in the Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act 1962. What the law says: A daughter inherits half the share of a son. A wife inherits 1/8 of husband's estate (if there are children) or 1/4 (if no children). These are the minimum legal shares — no one can be given less under Islamic law.

What families actually do (documented dispossession pattern): Family pressure on daughters to "waive" their inheritance share "for the sake of family unity" — sometimes with the claim this is religiously meritorious. Property is transferred only to sons before the father's death (as "gifts") to avoid the inheritance calculation. Daughters are told they already received their share through dowry (jahez) — which is not legally an inheritance substitute.

What the law actually provides: A daughter who is denied her legal inheritance share can file a civil suit in the Family Court or Civil Court. The Muslim Family Laws Ordinance 1961 and Succession Act 1925 provide legal frameworks for inheritance disputes. Women who know their legal rights and assert them win these cases. The problem is most don't know, and family pressure is enormous. HRCP and Aurat Foundation (aurat.org) provide legal support for women in inheritance disputes.

🚑 Abdul Sattar Edhi — The Complete Story
Source: Edhi's autobiography "A Mirror to the Blind," Edhi Foundation records, multiple biographies

The Man Who Built the World's Largest Volunteer Ambulance Service From Nothing — While Living in the Same Room for 60 Years

Abdul Sattar Edhi arrived in Karachi from Gujarat (India) in 1947 as a young man. He had almost nothing. He began by standing on a street corner with a donation box, collecting money for medicines for poor patients at Karachi's hospitals. He slept on the pavement.

How Edhi Foundation actually grew: Edhi's first dispensary (1951) was funded by community donations — he would stand in markets and collect. The Memon community of Karachi (his own community — Gujarati Muslim traders) provided initial support. But Edhi deliberately refused to limit himself to serving one community — he served Hindus, Christians, Shia, Sunni equally. This universality was controversial and threatened. Religious extremists repeatedly threatened him for burying unclaimed bodies of non-Muslims and sheltering women from all backgrounds in his homes.

The threats he faced: Edhi received death threats from religious groups who opposed his policy of burying unclaimed non-Muslim bodies with Islamic rites (the body has rights, he said — the religion of the dead person does not override the obligation to bury the body with dignity). He was accused by some clerics of "promoting immorality" for sheltering unwed mothers and abandoned children. He was investigated by Pakistani intelligence at various times for his connections across political and ethnic lines. He refused to be intimidated. He continued for 65 years.

What he built by the end: 1,800+ ambulances (Guinness World Record — world's largest volunteer ambulance fleet). Hospitals, clinics, maternity homes in every major Pakistani city. Rehabilitation centres for drug addicts. Homes for the mentally ill. Orphanages housing thousands of children. Mortuaries for unclaimed bodies — his morgues have identified and provided dignity to hundreds of thousands of nameless dead. All of this: built from voluntary donations. Zero government funding. Edhi refused government money because he said it came with conditions.

His personal life: Edhi lived in the same small room in Karachi for 60 years. He owned two pairs of clothes. He ate simply. When asked why he didn't live better given the organisation he ran, he said the money was not his — it belonged to the poor. He died in 2016 at age 88 or 89 (he was never sure of his exact birth year). At his funeral, an estimated 100,000 people came to pay respects. The government gave him a state funeral. He had refused state honours during his life.

The Faisal Edhi succession: His son Faisal Edhi now runs the foundation. Faisal has maintained the organisation's independence and non-discrimination principles. He has faced the same pressures his father faced — religious criticism, political pressure, funding challenges. The foundation continues, though critics note that without Edhi's personal charisma and moral authority, maintaining the organisational culture is harder.

⚡ Situation — What to Say Exactly

First words:

"Under what section of the law are you detaining me?"

This single question paralyses 90% of police who have no legal basis. If they cannot cite a specific law — you are free to leave. Stand your ground calmly.

If they try to take you:

"I am invoking Article 10 of the Constitution. I have the right to know the grounds of my arrest. Please state the specific offence and your badge number."

If they threaten the station:

"Under Section 61 CrPC you must produce me before a magistrate within 24 hours. I am noting the time now. It is [say time out loud]."
Take out your phone immediately and start recording. Say the date, time and location out loud. Note the officer's name and badge number visibly. Calm, slow voice — lower than theirs. Emotion loses. Calm authority wins.
"Do you have a search warrant signed by a Judicial Magistrate under Section 165 of the Code of Criminal Procedure? If not, I am not legally required to allow entry."

They cannot enter without it. Period. The only exception: hot pursuit of a fleeing suspect — which they must state explicitly with the suspect's name.

Keep the door partially closed. Do not invite them in. Do not panic. A calm refusal backed by the correct law section is much harder to override than an emotional refusal.

"Under Section 32 of the Prevention of Electronic Crimes Act 2016, you require a court warrant to access my electronic device. Do you have one?"

They almost never do. Say it calmly. Do not physically resist — but assert the legal requirement clearly and on record.

Before any situation escalates: enable the lock screen on your phone. Enable fingerprint only (so they can't compel you to unlock it under duress). Back up sensitive data to cloud.

"I am noting your name [say it], badge number [say it], and the time [say it]. Demanding money is an offence under Section 161 Pakistan Penal Code — punishable by up to 3 years imprisonment. I will be filing a complaint with the IG's office and Pakistan Citizen Portal."

Say it once. Clearly. Then actually note everything down visibly in your phone notes app in front of them. The act of documentation changes their calculation — they now know there is a record.

Then actually do it: File on Pakistan Citizen Portal (citizen.gov.pk) and IG Sindh complaint portal (021-99212120). Even if nothing happens immediately, the paper trail is real.

"I will not sign any document without my lawyer present. This is my right under Article 10A of the Constitution of Pakistan."

Do not sign ANYTHING. A blank paper. A "statement." A "confession." A "warning." Anything. Not without a lawyer. Many people are jailed in Pakistan on the basis of documents they signed under pressure without understanding.

If you have already been arrested and are being held: your right to a lawyer is immediate under Article 10A. Demand it repeatedly. Every time someone enters the room. Say: "I am requesting access to a lawyer under Article 10A. Please note this request." Make them acknowledge they heard it.
"Under what authority are you stopping me? If this is a traffic violation, please issue me an official challan under the Motor Vehicles Ordinance with your badge number and the specific violation stated."

No random "fees." Either a proper official challan — which creates a record they have to account for — or nothing. The e-challan system in Karachi means genuine challans are tracked. Unofficial payments are pure extortion.

"Vehicle impoundment requires a specific court order or a documented violation under Section 5 of the Motor Vehicles Act. Please state the specific violation and issue official documentation."

Suddenly files materialise. The RTI Act is one of the most underused legal tools available to ordinary Pakistanis.

Arif Hasan trained as an architect at the Architectural Association in London (1964) and has spent every subsequent decade in Karachi. He founded the Urban Resource Centre (URC) in 1989. He has advised the World Bank, UN-Habitat, Asian Development Bank, and governments across South Asia. He has written over 20 books on Pakistani urbanism. His work on the Orangi Pilot Project helped it become a global model replicated in over 15 countries. He has been right about almost everything that has happened to Karachi — and has been ignored by almost every government during that time. His research is free at urckarachi.org. Almost nobody under 30 knows it exists.

Killed for Documentation

Perween Rahman

30 years mapping Karachi's informal settlements and land grabs. Murdered March 13, 2013. Her gunmen were convicted. The people who ordered her killing were never prosecuted. The work continues.

Source: OPP-RTI records, ATC court documents, Dawn investigations, Human Rights Watch

Who She Was and What She Was Doing When She Was Killed

Perween Rahman joined the Orangi Pilot Project in 1983. She became its director in 1988 after Dr. Akhtar Hameed Khan's departure and ran it for 25 years. Under her leadership, OPP expanded from sewer construction to comprehensive community development: sanitation, housing, microfinance, education, health, and — crucially — land rights documentation.

In her final years she was building an evidence base mapping illegal land grabs in Orangi and surrounding katchi abadis — documenting which land had been illegally appropriated from residents, which officials had issued fraudulent leases, and which politically-connected individuals were behind specific land grabs. This documentation — maps, photographs, official records, testimonies — would have been the foundation for legal challenges to named individuals in Karachi's land mafia.

On March 13, 2013, armed men on motorcycles stopped her car near Banaras Colony as she was returning from a field visit. She was shot multiple times and died at the scene. She was 57 years old.

✅ What the Courts Did

An Anti-Terrorism Court convicted three men — Rehman alias Gullu, Mohammad Ameen alias Bhura, and Rizwan alias Chhota Bhura — all linked to a land mafia network. They received death sentences subject to appeal. This is genuine accountability for the direct perpetrators, achieved after years of civil society pressure.

❌ What the Courts Didn't Do

The intellectual authors of the murder — the land mafia figures who commissioned it — have never been prosecuted. Investigative journalism by Dawn and others named individuals with documented connections to Karachi's land market. The evidence exists. The political will to prosecute it has never existed. This is the standard pattern for targeted killings of civil society figures in Pakistan: the gunmen are convicted, the patrons walk free.

"Perween Rahman was not killed because she was building sewers. She was killed because she was building evidence. The land she was documenting was worth billions. OPP-RTI continues that documentation today — the same work that made her a target."
OPP-RTI continues operating in Orangi under director Aquila Ismail. Research available at oppinstitutions.org

Building Despite Everything

Karachi's Tech Founders

They built Pakistan's most significant tech companies from Karachi — during load shedding, regulatory chaos, and an economic system designed to make building hard. Their stories and what they actually say about working here.

Founded 2017 · Motorcycle ride-hailing + delivery

🛵 Bykea — Muneeb Mufti

Bykea digitised the motorcycle economy that already moved millions of Karachites daily. While Uber and Careem served the car-owning minority, Bykea saw the 70% of commuters who use motorcycles. Their specific insight: the informal motorcycle taxi system already existed — it just needed a digital layer for safety, pricing, and reliability. What Mufti has said publicly about building here: the talent exists, the market is enormous, but generator costs, inconsistent banking infrastructure, and payment system gaps add 30-40% to operating costs vs equivalent companies in other markets. Bykea survived where better-funded competitors (Airlift, Swvl) failed by focusing on the mass market that upper-middle-class founders typically overlook.

Founded 2021 · First SBP-licensed digital bank

💳 NayaPay — Usman Ashraf

Pakistan has approximately 100 million financially excluded adults — people who have never had a bank account. NayaPay, founded by Usman Ashraf, became Pakistan's first fully licensed neobank approved by the State Bank of Pakistan — offering full banking services digitally without branch infrastructure. What Ashraf has said about the regulatory environment: "Licensing took longer than building the product. But once done, you have a genuine moat — the regulatory barrier that almost destroyed us became our protection." The SBP's e-money licensing framework — one of Pakistan's genuine regulatory successes — made NayaPay possible.

Founded 2020 · B2B retail commerce

📦 Bazaar — Hamza Amin + Saif Ahmed

Bazaar digitised Pakistan's retail supply chain — connecting manufacturers to the millions of kiryana (corner) stores that run on cash and informal credit. Founded in Karachi, raised ~$30M Series A in 2021 (one of Pakistan's largest). Then: the 2022 global funding winter combined with Pakistan's economic crisis forced major layoffs and restructuring. Bazaar's story is both inspiring and cautionary — Pakistani startups face local challenges (load shedding, regulatory friction) AND global capital market exposure that their limited domestic investor base cannot buffer. The company restructured and continues. What its experience shows: building in Pakistan requires planning for both local crises and international ones simultaneously.

The common thread across all founders

🏙️ What Building in Karachi Is Like

Across public interviews, all Karachi founders cite the same pattern: Power costs — generator operating costs are a significant expense that Karachi startups bear that London or Singapore startups don't. Talent flight — their best engineers are continuously recruited by remote-first international companies at 3-5× local salaries. Companies build talent and watch it leave. Banking friction — receiving foreign investment, processing international payments, navigating SBP regulations adds weeks to processes that should take hours. The opportunity they all see: 220 million people, rapidly growing smartphone penetration, massive unmet need for financial services, logistics, healthcare — and almost no incumbent digital infrastructure. "The problems are enormous. That's why the opportunity is enormous."

EST
The Word Everyone Uses — Nobody Explains

The Establishment

What does "the establishment" actually mean in Pakistan? Who is in it? How does it make decisions? How does it communicate with civilian government? Why does it persist across every election? The mechanical explanation — not conspiracy, documented institutional reality.

Source: Ayesha Siddiqa "Military Inc." 2007, Husain Haqqani "Pakistan: Between Mosque and Military" 2005, IMF GCDA 2025, academic political science literature

The Mechanical Definition

In Pakistani political discourse, "the establishment" means the military-intelligence complex — specifically: Pakistan Army General Headquarters (GHQ) Rawalpindi, Inter-Services Intelligence (ISI), and the senior military leadership (COAS, corps commanders). It does not formally include: elected government, Parliament, or the judiciary — though relationships between all of these and the establishment are documented and significant.

Why it has power beyond its formal role: Pakistan's Army has governed directly for approximately half of its 75-year existence. In civilian periods it maintains indirect influence through: control of foreign and nuclear security policy (which it has never relinquished to civilians); selective accountability — using NAB and FIA to accelerate or stall cases based on political calculus; economic interests (military businesses worth $50-100B+ by 2025 estimates) that require political protection; and ISI's political wing which maintains relationships across all parties simultaneously.

The khaki business empire 2025: Ayesha Siddiqa's 2007 estimate was $20B. Since then: Fauji Foundation (fertiliser, food, energy — publicly listed companies), Army Welfare Trust (Askari Bank, Askari Cement, insurance), DHA across all major cities (revenue not subject to civilian audit), and dozens of subsidiary entities. The IMF's 2025 GCDA report specifically identified military business immunity from civilian oversight as a governance failure. Military businesses operate under military authority — no SECP oversight, no independent audit, no civilian financial disclosure.

The Dawn Leaks (2016): Dawn published a story about a civilian-military meeting where civilian officials told the military that militant groups operating from Pakistani soil were damaging Pakistan's international relationships. ISPR denied accuracy, demanded retraction. Dawn refused. The journalist (Cyril Almeida) was placed on the Exit Control List — barred from leaving Pakistan — then removed after international pressure. This is the most documented recent case of establishment pressure on civilian journalism.

PEMRA channel actions: Pakistan's broadcast regulator has issued off-air notices to TV channels including Geo News during tension periods. Cable operators have been pressured to drop specific channels. All documented in Freedom House, RSF, and CPJ reports.

The 2024 election internet shutdown: On election day February 8, 2024, PTA shut down mobile internet across Pakistan — disrupting independent parallel result tabulation. Pakistan was the only country to shut down national internet on an election day in 2024. The government cited "security." Independent observers cited the specific timing: internet was restored only after initial result tabulation was complete.

The fundamental reason: The Army controls Pakistan's nuclear weapons and primary external security function. No civilian government has the leverage to fundamentally subordinate an institution with strategic weapons and 75 years of operational independence. This is not unique to Pakistan — civilian control of nuclear-armed militaries is difficult everywhere — but in Pakistan the institutional culture, economic interests, and political networks are more deeply embedded than in most comparable countries.

The enabling factor: Pakistan's civilian institutions are weak — courts have historically been reluctant to challenge military decisions; Parliament has never successfully passed legislation meaningfully constraining military prerogatives; political parties are personality-based, making them manageable through individual incentives. A genuine civilian institutional counterweight has never been built — partly because the military prevents it, partly because individual civilian politicians often find accommodation personally beneficial.

What has actually shifted the terrain (slowly): External pressure (US conditions on military aid post-2001 and 2011); economic crisis (IMF governance conditions creating external accountability pressure); and sustained popular mobilisation (PTI's mass movement changed the political cost calculation even if it was ultimately contained). None produce immediate change. All gradually shift what is possible.

Social Secrets

👶 Child Marriage — Urban Karachi, Not Just Rural Pakistan
Source: UNICEF Pakistan, HRCP Annual Reports 2024, Girls Not Brides Pakistan

21% of Pakistani Girls Married Before 18 — Including in Karachi's Low-Income Communities

Pakistan has the world's 6th highest number of child brides. While disproportionately rural, child marriage occurs in Karachi's low-income areas — particularly in Lyari (Baloch community customs), Orangi and Baldia (Afghan and tribal practices), and katchi abadis where poverty creates economic pressure. The Sindh Child Marriage Restraint Act 2013 raised the minimum age to 18 for both genders. Enforcement: near zero. HRCP 2022 found almost no successful prosecutions. Police treat it as a "family matter." Nikah registrars rarely verify ages. Forged birth certificates are available and cheap. In extreme poverty, marriage transfers a daughter's maintenance costs and may be the family's only access to cash (mehr). This is not culture. It is economic desperation produced by the same governance failures documented throughout this guide.

🔪 Honour Killing — Urban Cases, Near-Zero Convictions
Source: HRCP 2024 Annual Report, Aurat Foundation data, Dawn crime investigations

400-500 Documented Honour Killings Per Year — Real Number 2-3× Higher

HRCP documents 400-500 honour killings annually in Pakistan. Actual numbers are higher: many are registered as accidents; families don't report against family members; police treat them as private matters. Urban cases occur in Karachi — particularly in communities with strong rural-origin connections. The Criminal Law Amendment Act 2016 closed the "compounding" loophole that allowed families to forgive killers. Even so: conviction rates remain extremely low. Prosecutors face witness intimidation. Police registration is incomplete. Some judges apply sympathetic interpretations. The legal reform was real. Its enforcement is not. Organisations: War Against Rape Karachi: 021-35388700. Shirkat Gah: 021-35840894. HRCP: hrcp-web.org.

🏠 Domestic Workers — 3 Million People, Zero Legal Protection
Source: ILO Pakistan, Human Rights Watch "Swept Under the Rug" 2020, PBS Labour Force Survey

Pakistan Has No National Domestic Worker Law — HRW Called It "Forced Labour" for Many

Pakistan has 8-12 million domestic workers nationally; Karachi's share approximately 2-3 million. They are explicitly excluded from Pakistan's Labour Laws. No minimum wage. No maximum working hours. No sick leave. No maternity protection. No protection against arbitrary dismissal. HRW's 2020 report documented: workers locked in homes, physical abuse, confiscated identity documents, non-payment of wages, sexual harassment. The employers are often ordinary middle-class families — not exceptional people, but people operating in a system with zero oversight. Sindh passed a Domestic Workers Act in 2020 — the first in Pakistan. It requires: written contract, weekly day off, 14 days annual leave, 10 sick days, maternity leave. Most employers don't know this law exists. Most workers don't know they have rights under it. Pakistan has not ratified ILO Convention 189 on Domestic Workers.

💰 How Pakistan's Elite Pay Almost Zero Tax — The Specific Mechanisms

Agricultural income tax is a provincial subject under Pakistan's Constitution. All four provinces maintain near-zero agricultural income tax. Result: the families owning hundreds of thousands of acres of prime agricultural land — the same families dominating the National Assembly, Senate, and provincial cabinets — pay almost zero income tax on their largest income source.

FBR data: total agricultural income tax collected: less than Rs 3 billion annually. Agricultural sector GDP contribution: ~24% (roughly Rs 18 trillion). If taxed at salaried rates, revenue would be in hundreds of billions. A Karachi salaried worker earning Rs 100,000/month pays Rs 8,000-12,000/month automatically deducted. A feudal landlord with tens of millions in annual agricultural income: pays thousands, if at all. Every IMF programme has conditioned meaningful agricultural income tax. Every programme: the landlord class that controls Parliament has blocked it. Consistently for 30+ years.

Pakistan has two property valuation systems: DC rates (official government valuations used for tax) and market rates (actual transaction prices). In most of Karachi, DC rates are 10-30% of actual market values — by design. This allows properties to be registered at DC rates (minimal stamp duty and CGT) while the actual cash consideration is much higher, paid outside the banking system.

A property selling for Rs 50M in DHA is registered at Rs 10-15M. Rs 35-40M in undeclared cash moves outside the formal economy — entering the property market from drug money, corruption proceeds, or other illicit funds — with minimal tax consequence. FBR introduced "FBR valuation tables" (2016) with more realistic values. Still lagging market rates. Still inconsistently enforced. The DHA plot file market specifically functions as a currency for undeclared wealth — files are bought, held, and sold between parties as a store of black money, with the price appreciation itself creating taxable gains that are never reported.

The Pattern That Explains the Impunity

CPJ analysis of Pakistan journalist murders shows a consistent pattern: journalists covering intelligence-militant relationships, military operations, ethnic violence, and political party criminal networks are at highest risk. The institutions that would prosecute their killers — police, intelligence agencies, courts — are often the same institutions implicated in, or protective of, those responsible for the killings.

The Wali Khan Babar case is the most documented example of how witness intimidation prevents prosecution: after his 2011 murder (investigating MQM-linked crime in Karachi), multiple prosecution witnesses were themselves killed before the trial could proceed. Three men were convicted — later challenged. When the witnesses to a journalist's murder are themselves murdered, the impunity is structural, not incidental.

📋 Pakistan's Current IMF EFF Programme — The Actual Conditions
Source: IMF Extended Fund Facility Pakistan (approved July 2023, extended 2024), IMF staff reports, publicly available at imf.org

What Pakistan Has Legally Agreed To Do — And What It Has Actually Done

Pakistan's $7B Extended Fund Facility (EFF) approved July 2023 and extended in 2024 contains specific structural benchmarks and quantitative performance criteria. These are legally binding conditions for disbursement. Key conditions and status:

✅ Broadly met: Exchange rate flexibility — Pakistan allowed the rupee to find market levels (from Rs 220/$ artificial peg to Rs 270-285/$). Painful for consumers. Necessary for reserves stabilisation.

✅ Broadly met: Energy sector reforms — Electricity and gas tariff increases implemented. Politically costly. K-Electric and SNGPL tariffs raised significantly.

⚠️ Partially met: Agricultural income tax — IMF required provinces to pass agricultural income tax legislation by December 2024. Legislation was passed in modified form. Rates remain extremely low. IMF expressed "concern" but continued disbursements.

❌ Not met: Military business civilian oversight — IMF GCDA (November 2025) explicitly called for subjecting military businesses to civilian regulatory oversight. Pakistan acknowledged the concern. No legislation passed. No regulatory change implemented.

❌ Not met: SOE privatisation — Pakistan Steel Mills, PIA, DISCOs on privatisation list. PIA sold in reduced form. Steel Mills: not sold. DISCOs: partially restructured. Full privatisation targets missed.

The fundamental problem: Pakistan has completed 23 IMF programmes since 1958. It is one of the most frequent IMF borrowers globally. It has never successfully implemented a programme that produced lasting self-sufficiency. The structural reasons — agricultural tax exemption for the ruling class, military business exemptions from civilian oversight, energy circular debt that rebuilds after each "resolution" — remain intact across every programme.

📊 The Foreign Exchange Reserves — What's Real
Source: State Bank of Pakistan weekly reserve reports, IMF Article IV consultations, academic analysis

SBP Reports X. The Real Usable Amount Is Y. The Difference Matters.

SBP reports "official foreign exchange reserves" weekly. As of late 2025, headline number: approximately $10-12B. What this headline number hides:

Currency swap arrangements: A portion of reserves is borrowed from Saudi Arabia, UAE, and China through currency swap or bilateral deposit arrangements. These arrangements are repayable on demand or at maturity. They look like reserves on the balance sheet but cannot be freely used for import payments without triggering a demand for repayment.

Commercial bank holdings: SBP reserves include commercial bank foreign currency holdings that the central bank holds on their behalf. These cannot be used for SBP's own purposes without creating a banking system liability.

The IMF disbursements: A significant portion of current reserves is IMF money — which must be repaid on the IMF schedule. It is borrowed reserves, not earned reserves.

The "usable" reserves figure — actual liquid foreign exchange the SBP can freely deploy — is consistently significantly lower than the headline number. In the 2022-23 crisis, Pakistan's usable reserves reportedly fell below $4B (less than one month of import cover) while the headline number showed $8-9B. The gap between headline and usable is deliberately not publicised by the government, because acknowledging it would accelerate the crisis it is trying to prevent.
